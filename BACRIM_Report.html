1. Introduction

1.1 Context and Motivation

Organized crime operates as a relational system where alliances and rivalries shape power, coordination, and conflict among criminal organizations. In Mexico, drug cartels form complex webs of cooperation and competition that determine territorial control, operational capacity, and the dynamics of violence. Between 2012 and 2021, Mexico recorded over 290,000 homicides and over 175,000 missing persons — figures that correlate closely with cartel fragmentation and inter-organizational conflict (INEGI, 2022).

The BACRIM 2020 dataset (Prieto-Curiel & Campedelli, 2023) represents a signed network capturing both positive ties (alliances) and negative ties (rivalries) among 150 Mexican criminal organizations as of 2020. Unlike many criminal network datasets that focus on individual actors, BACRIM operates at the organizational level, treating each cartel, cell, or local group as a node. This allows us to study the macro-structure of criminal cooperation - who allies with whom, who competes against whom, and which organizations serve as bridges or brokers.

Network science provides a natural framework for this analysis. Rather than treating each cartel as an isolated actor, we model the full relational structure as a graph \(G = (V, E)\), where vertices \(V\) represent organizations and edges \(E\) represent observed alliances or rivalries. This formalism captures dependencies, brokerage, clustering, and potential future link formation - dimensions that are invisible to traditional organizational charts or case-by-case intelligence analysis (Sparrow, 1991).

We enrich the network data with organizational specialties - the primary criminal activities (drug trafficking, extortion, fuel theft, kidnapping) and drug portfolios (fentanyl, methamphetamine, cocaine, heroin) of each cartel - drawn from DEA National Drug Threat Assessments (U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration, 2024, 2025), Congressional Research Service reports, and InSight Crime profiles. This allows us to test whether alliance formation is driven by functional complementarity (cartels with different specialties allying to form integrated supply chains) or homophily (cartels with similar activities preferring to cooperate).

Events between 2024 and 2026 — the arrest of Ismael “El Mayo” Zambada, the killing of CJNG leader “El Mencho,” and the US designation of both cartels as terrorist organizations — track the structural vulnerabilities identified from this 2020 snapshot with notable precision. We discuss these connections in Section 4.5.

1.2 Research Questions

The research questions we are trying to answer in this document are:

  • What are the key structural properties of the cartel alliance network, and how do organizational characteristics (type, specialty, drug portfolio) relate to network position?
  • Is the observed alliance network compatible with a random process, or does it show structured organization?
  • Which currently disconnected cartel pairs are most likely to form future alliances, and do non-topological features (specialty, rivalry, geography) improve prediction over topology alone?
  • How does the network structure change when a critical hub node is removed, and how does the surviving network plausibly reorganize?
  • Given a limited budget of \(k\) simultaneous removals, which target set minimizes the largest connected component after the network rewires?

1.3 Core Analytical Objectives

Beyond the descriptive and comparative analyses above, this report focuses on two main investigative objectives:

  1. Finding Hidden Connections in the Network (Link Prediction): Not all relationships between criminal organizations are directly observable. Two cartels that are not currently allied may nonetheless share many mutual allies, operate in adjacent territories, or occupy structurally similar positions in the network. Link prediction algorithms exploit these patterns to identify the most plausible hidden or future connections - pairs of organizations that do not yet have a direct alliance but are topologically primed to form one.

We use two complementary approaches: the Jaccard coefficient, which measures the fraction of shared neighbors between two nodes, and the Adamic-Adar index, which weights shared neighbors by their exclusivity (rare shared connections are more informative than common ones). Together, these methods reveal the “invisible wiring” of the cartel ecosystem - alliances that likely exist but are unrecorded, or that are likely to emerge in the near future.

  1. Network Resilience: What Happens When a Critical Node Is Removed? Criminal networks, like many real-world networks, are heterogeneous - a small number of highly connected hubs hold together a large periphery of smaller organizations. This raises a critical strategic questions: what happens if a hub is eliminated? Does the network fragment into isolated clusters, or does it remain largely intact?

We simulate the targeted removal of the most central node (CJNG / El Mencho) and measure the resulting structural disruption: changes in connectivity, fragmentation into components, increased path lengths, and loss of cohesion. We extend this to a sequential attack on the top-5 hubs to trace the full fragmentation curve. This analysis has direct real-world relevance: the February 2026 killing of El Mencho and the 2024 arrest of El Mayo Zambada are precisely the kind of hub removals our simulation models, allowing us to validate network-theoretic predictions against observed outcomes.

1.5 Literature Review

The application of network science to organized crime has grown substantially over the past two decades. Below we review key contributions that inform our analytical framework.

Criminal Networks as Complex Systems

Morselli (2009) established the foundational framework for studying criminal organizations as networks in Inside Criminal Networks. He showed that criminal groups exhibit structural properties — degree heterogeneity, clustering, and brokerage — that reflect strategic coordination rather than randomness. Centrality measures (degree and betweenness) can identify leaders and brokers in covert networks, even when intelligence is incomplete.

Sparrow (1991) was among the first to argue that social network analysis provides law enforcement with tools superior to hierarchy-based organizational charts. He identified three challenges of criminal network analysis that remain relevant: incompleteness (missing nodes and edges), fuzzy boundaries (unclear group membership), and dynamics (networks evolve over time).

Signed Networks and Structural Balance

Our dataset contains both alliance (+) and rivalry (−) ties, making it a signed network. The theoretical basis for analyzing such structures comes from Heider (1946)’s balance theory and its graph-theoretic formalization by Cartwright & Harary (1956): in a balanced network, “the enemy of my enemy is my friend” and “the friend of my friend is my friend.” Leskovec et al. (2010) extended balance theory to large-scale online networks and found that real signed networks tend toward structural balance — a pattern relevant to interpreting cartel alliance and rivalry structures.

Network Robustness and Targeted Attacks

Albert et al. (2000) demonstrated that scale-free networks (those with heavy-tailed degree distributions) are robust to random node failures but highly vulnerable to targeted attacks on hubs. Arresting random low-level operatives has minimal impact on network connectivity; targeting the most connected nodes causes disproportionate fragmentation. Callaway et al. (2000) provided the complementary percolation-theory framework for understanding these phase transitions in network connectivity.

Duijn et al. (2014) applied these robustness ideas to criminal networks specifically, finding that targeting brokers (high betweenness) is often more effective than targeting hubs (high degree), because broker removal fragments communication channels even when fewer edges are lost.

Link Prediction in Criminal Networks

Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg (2007) established the theoretical framework for topological link prediction, comparing common neighbors, Jaccard coefficient, and Adamic-Adar index. They showed that network topology alone — without external features — can predict future edges with significant accuracy.

Berlusconi et al. (2016) applied link prediction to Italian Mafia co-offending networks and found that Jaccard and Adamic-Adar metrics identified hidden connections that intelligence agencies later confirmed. Shared allies create structural pressure toward alliance formation.

Mexican Cartel Networks Specifically

Prieto-Curiel & Campedelli (2023) — the source of our BACRIM dataset — provided the first comprehensive signed network of Mexican cartel alliances and rivalries at the organizational level. They documented 150 organizations across Mexican states and showed that the network exhibits community structure aligned with geographic regions.

Coscia & Rios (2012) analyzed the evolution of Mexican cartel territories over time, finding that violence and territorial fragmentation follow patterns consistent with network competition models. Calderón et al. (2015) studied how Mexico’s “kingpin strategy” (targeting cartel leaders) produced network fragmentation that paradoxically increased violence — a finding directly relevant to our node removal analysis.

2.1 Data and Preprocessing

We use five data files, three from the BACRIM 2020 dataset and two supplementary:

Network data (Prieto-Curiel & Campedelli, 2023):

  • BACRIM2020_Nodes.csv — 150 cartel organizations with group name, state, and short name.
  • BACRIM2020_Alliances.csv — Alliance edges with positive weights (weight = number of Mexican states where the alliance is active).
  • BACRIM2020_Rivals.csv — Rivalry edges with negative weights (weight = number of states where the rivalry is active).

Organizational specialty data (compiled from DEA NDTA 2024/2025, CRS report R41576, and InSight Crime profiles (Congressional Research Service, 2024; InSight Crime, 2026; U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration, 2024, 2025)):

Temporal context data (INEGI, 2022):

  • Trends2012_2021.csv — Annual counts of homicides, missing persons, and arrests in Mexico from 2012 to 2021, providing macro-level context for the violence landscape in which the BACRIM network operates.

# --- Deduplicate edges (each edge appears twice, once per direction) ---
def deduplicate_edges(df):
    """Canonicalize edges so (min_node, max_node) is always used."""
    edges = []
    seen = set()
    for _, row in df.iterrows():
        u, v = row["Node"], row["RNode"]
        key = (min(u, v), max(u, v))
        if key not in seen:
            seen.add(key)
            edges.append({"Node": u, "RNode": v, "weight": row["weight"]})
    return pd.DataFrame(edges)

2.3 Topic 1: Foundations of Networks

We compute fundamental network metrics on the alliance graph. These metrics characterize the global topology (how dense, how far apart are nodes, how clustered are connections) and identify the most important individual actors.

How Do We Determine the Importance of a Node?

In network science, “importance” is not a single concept — different centrality measures capture different dimensions of a node’s structural role. We use two complementary measures:

Degree Centrality — Who Has the Most Connections?

Intuition. The simplest way to measure a node’s importance is to count how many connections it has. A cartel allied with 20 others is more “important” (in the sense of reach and coordination capacity) than one allied with 2.

Formal definition. For a node \(v\) in an undirected graph \(G = (V, E)\) with \(N = |V|\) nodes, the degree \(k_v\) is the number of edges incident to \(v\):

\[k_v = \sum_{j \in V} A_{vj}\]

where \(A\) is the adjacency matrix (\(A_{vj} = 1\) if \((v,j) \in E\), else 0). The degree centrality normalizes this by the maximum possible connections:

\[C_D(v) = \frac{k_v}{N - 1}\]

The value ranges from 0 (isolated node) to 1 (connected to every other node).

In the cartel context: a high degree centrality means the organization maintains alliances with many other cartels simultaneously. This makes it a hub — a central coordinator in the cooperation network. CJNG, for example, has the highest degree centrality because it is allied with more organizations than any other cartel. Removing a high-degree node severs many connections at once, which is why law enforcement targets these hubs.

Algorithmic complexity: Computing degree centrality for all nodes is \(O(N + E)\) — simply count the edges per node and normalize.

Betweenness Centrality — Who Controls the Flow?

Intuition. Even a node with few connections can be critically important if it lies on the only path between two otherwise separate groups. Think of a small cartel that is the sole intermediary between a production hub and a distribution network — removing it disconnects the entire supply chain.

Formal definition. Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on the shortest paths between other pairs of nodes. For a node \(v\):

\[C_B(v) = \sum_{\substack{s \neq v \neq t \\ s, t \in V}} \frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}\]

where \(\sigma_{st}\) is the total number of shortest paths from node \(s\) to node \(t\), and \(\sigma_{st}(v)\) is the number of those shortest paths that pass through \(v\). In NetworkX, this value is normalized by dividing by \(\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}\) for undirected graphs, so it falls in \([0, 1]\).

In the cartel context: a high betweenness centrality identifies brokers or bridge organizations — cartels that connect otherwise separate clusters. Even if a cartel has relatively few direct alliances (moderate degree), it can have high betweenness if it is the only path linking two groups of cartels. Removing such a broker fragments the network into disconnected components.

Algorithm: Brandes’ method. Computing betweenness centrality naively requires enumerating all shortest paths — \(O(N^3)\) for unweighted graphs. Brandes (2001) developed an efficient algorithm that runs in \(O(NE)\) time:

  1. For each source node \(s\), perform a BFS to compute shortest-path distances and counts \(\sigma_{st}\) for all targets \(t\).
  2. In a single backward pass (processing nodes in reverse BFS order), accumulate a dependency score \(\delta_s(v) = \sum_{t: v \text{ is on } s{\text{-}}t \text{ path}} \frac{\sigma_{sv}}{\sigma_{st}} (1 + \delta_s(t))\).
  3. Sum the dependency scores: \(C_B(v) = \sum_s \delta_s(v)\).

This avoids explicitly enumerating all \(O(N^2)\) shortest paths and instead propagates dependency information through the BFS tree.

Why Use Both Measures Together?

Scenario Degree Betweenness Interpretation
High degree, high betweenness High High Dominant hub — many allies AND a bridge (e.g., CJNG)
High degree, low betweenness High Low Local hub — many allies but within a dense cluster; redundant paths exist around it
Low degree, high betweenness Low High Critical bridge — few allies but connects otherwise separated groups; removal causes fragmentation
Low degree, low betweenness Low Low Peripheral node — few allies, easily bypassed; removal has minimal impact

A node that ranks high on both measures (like CJNG in this network) is the most strategically important target: it is both highly connected and structurally irreplaceable. Conversely, a node that ranks high on betweenness but not degree represents a hidden vulnerability — its removal would fragment the network despite its seemingly modest alliance portfolio.

This dual-centrality framework directly informs our targeted node removal simulation in Section 2.6: we select CJNG as the removal target precisely because it dominates both rankings.

2.4 Topic 2: Random Graph Models

A central question in network analysis is whether the observed structure could have arisen by chance. To answer this, we compare the empirical alliance network against two null models — theoretical random graph constructions that serve as baselines.

The Erdős–Rényi Model \(G(N, p)\)

Intuition. The simplest random graph: take \(N\) nodes and independently connect each pair with probability \(p\). Every possible edge is equally likely, and there is no preferential attachment, clustering, or community structure by construction.

Formal definition (Erdős & Rényi, 1959). Given \(N\) nodes, each of the \(\binom{N}{2}\) possible edges exists independently with probability \(p\). The expected number of edges is \(\mathbb{E}[E] = \binom{N}{2} p\) and the expected degree of each node is:

\[\langle k \rangle = (N-1)p\]

The degree distribution follows a Poisson distribution for large \(N\):

\[P(k) \approx \frac{\langle k \rangle^k}{k!} e^{-\langle k \rangle}\]

This means all nodes have roughly similar degree — there are no “hubs.” The clustering coefficient converges to \(C_{\text{ER}} = p = \frac{\langle k \rangle}{N-1}\), which is typically very small for sparse networks.

Why is ER a useful null model? It represents the hypothesis that alliances form at random with no structural logic. If the real network has significantly higher clustering or different degree distribution than ER, we can reject this hypothesis and conclude that alliances are governed by some organizing principle (trust, geography, complementary specialization).

To match our data, we set \(p = \frac{2E_{\text{real}}}{N(N-1)}\) so that the ER graph has the same expected number of edges as the empirical alliance network.

The Barabási–Albert Model (Preferential Attachment)

Intuition. In many real-world networks, new nodes do not connect randomly — they prefer to link to already well-connected nodes. This “rich get richer” mechanism, called preferential attachment, produces hub-dominated networks with heavy-tailed degree distributions.

Formal definition (Barabási & Albert, 1999). Start with \(m_0\) connected nodes. At each time step, a new node arrives and connects to \(m\) existing nodes with probability proportional to their current degree:

\[\Pi(k_i) = \frac{k_i}{\sum_j k_j}\]

A node with degree \(k\) is \(k\) times more likely to attract new connections than a node with degree 1. The resulting degree distribution follows a power law:

\[P(k) \sim k^{-\gamma}, \quad \gamma = 3\]

In log-log space, this appears as a straight line with slope \(-3\). The clustering coefficient of BA graphs is higher than ER but still lower than most real-world networks.

Why is BA a useful comparison? If the real cartel network shows heavy-tailed degree distribution (a few hubs with many alliances, many groups with few), this is consistent with a preferential attachment mechanism: new or expanding cartels preferentially ally with already-powerful organizations, reinforcing their dominance.

2.4.3 Small-World Analysis

A network is said to exhibit the small-world property (Watts & Strogatz, 1998) if it has:

  1. High clustering relative to a random graph: \(C_{\text{real}} \gg C_{\text{ER}}\)
  2. Short path lengths comparable to a random graph: \(L_{\text{real}} \approx L_{\text{ER}}\)

The small-world coefficient \(\sigma\) formalizes this:

\[\sigma = \frac{C_{\text{real}} / C_{\text{ER}}}{L_{\text{real}} / L_{\text{ER}}}\]

A value \(\sigma > 1\) indicates small-world properties. Intuitively, a small-world network has dense local clusters (allies of allies tend to be allies) but also enough “shortcuts” (cross-cluster alliances) to keep all nodes close in terms of path length.

2.5 Topic 3: Link Prediction

We predict future alliances among currently non-adjacent pairs in the alliance graph using two topological similarity metrics. The fundamental idea, formalized by Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg (2007), is that two nodes sharing many common neighbors are likely to form a direct connection in the future — the “friends of friends” principle.

Algorithm: Jaccard Coefficient

Intuition. The Jaccard coefficient measures the proportion of neighbors that two nodes share, relative to their total combined neighborhood. It penalizes pairs where one node has many neighbors but shares few of them with the other.

Formal definition. For two non-adjacent nodes \(u\) and \(v\) with neighborhoods \(\mathcal{N}(u)\) and \(\mathcal{N}(v)\):

\[J(u, v) = \frac{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)|}{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cup \mathcal{N}(v)|}\]

The value ranges from 0 (no shared neighbors) to 1 (identical neighborhoods). In the cartel context, \(J = 0.5\) between two organizations means that half of their combined alliance portfolio overlaps — a strong topological signal that a direct alliance is plausible.

Computational complexity. For a single pair, computing \(J\) requires set intersection and union of neighbor lists: \(O(\min(k_u, k_v))\) with sorted adjacency lists. For all non-edges: \(O(N^2 \cdot \bar{k})\).

Algorithm: Adamic-Adar Index

Intuition. Not all shared neighbors are equally informative. If two cartels share a common ally that itself has 50 alliances, this is less meaningful (the common ally allies with everyone) than if they share a common ally that has only 3 alliances (a selective, exclusive connection). Adamic-Adar weights each shared neighbor inversely by the logarithm of its degree.

Formal definition (Adamic & Adar, 2003). For two non-adjacent nodes \(u\) and \(v\):

\[AA(u, v) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)} \frac{1}{\log |\mathcal{N}(w)|}\]

Each shared neighbor \(w\) contributes \(\frac{1}{\log k_w}\) to the score. Low-degree shared neighbors contribute more because they represent selective, informative connections. A shared ally with degree 2 contributes \(\frac{1}{\log 2} \approx 1.44\), while one with degree 100 contributes only \(\frac{1}{\log 100} \approx 0.22\).

Why Adamic-Adar complements Jaccard. Jaccard treats all shared neighbors equally, while Adamic-Adar gives more weight to rare connections. In practice, the top-ranked predictions by both metrics are often similar, but Adamic-Adar can surface pairs connected through exclusive intermediaries that Jaccard would undervalue.

2.5.3 Dyadic Utility Model

Jaccard and Adamic-Adar use topology alone. They cannot distinguish between two pairs that share the same number of neighbors but differ in every other dimension — different specialties, different states, different positions in the rivalry network. A more complete model of alliance formation treats each potential pair as a decision: two organizations ally when the structural, functional, and strategic conditions are right.

We frame this as a logistic regression on dyads. For every pair \((i, j)\) among the 91 non-isolate nodes, the response is 1 if an alliance edge exists and 0 otherwise. The feature vector captures six dimensions of dyadic affinity:

Feature Definition Rationale
SharedAllies Common neighbors in alliance graph Triadic closure: mutual allies create trust
SharedRivals Common enemies in signed graph Balance theory: shared enemies push toward alliance
Complementarity 1 if primary specialties differ, 0 otherwise Supply-chain logic: different capabilities attract
SameState 1 if both in the same Mexican state Geographic proximity enables cooperation
BalanceScore Sum over common signed neighbors: +1 if both ties have same sign, -1 otherwise Heider balance in the signed network
DegreeProduct \(\deg(i) \times \deg(j)\) in alliance graph Preferential attachment: hubs attract more ties

The model is:

\[P(\text{alliance} \mid i, j) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_{ij})}}\]

With 91 edges among \(\binom{91}{2} = 4{,}095\) possible pairs, the positive rate is 2.2%. We use class-weighted logistic regression (class_weight='balanced' in scikit-learn) to prevent the model from trivially predicting all zeros. Each coefficient has a direct interpretation: \(\beta_k > 0\) means that feature \(k\) increases the probability of alliance, and \(\exp(\beta_k)\) gives the odds ratio.

def compute_dyadic_features(u, v, G_all, G_riv, G_sign):
    """Compute 6 dyadic features for a node pair."""
    # SharedAllies
    nu = set(G_all.neighbors(u)) if G_all.has_node(u) else set()
    nv = set(G_all.neighbors(v)) if G_all.has_node(v) else set()
    shared_allies = len(nu & nv)

    # SharedRivals
    ru = set(G_riv.neighbors(u)) if G_riv.has_node(u) else set()
    rv = set(G_riv.neighbors(v)) if G_riv.has_node(v) else set()
    shared_rivals = len(ru & rv)

    # Complementarity (1 if different specialties, both documented)
    su = node_specialty.get(u, "Undocumented")
    sv = node_specialty.get(v, "Undocumented")
    complementarity = int(su != sv and su != "Undocumented" and sv != "Undocumented")

    # SameState
    same_state = int(node_state.get(u, "") == node_state.get(v, "")
                     and node_state.get(u, "") != "")

    # BalanceScore from signed graph
    balance = 0
    sign_neighbors_u = set(G_sign.neighbors(u)) if G_sign.has_node(u) else set()
    sign_neighbors_v = set(G_sign.neighbors(v)) if G_sign.has_node(v) else set()
    for w in sign_neighbors_u & sign_neighbors_v:
        wt_uw = G_sign[u][w].get("weight", 0)
        wt_vw = G_sign[v][w].get("weight", 0)
        balance += 1 if wt_uw * wt_vw > 0 else -1

    # DegreeProduct
    du = G_all.degree(u) if G_all.has_node(u) else 0
    dv = G_all.degree(v) if G_all.has_node(v) else 0
    deg_product = du * dv

    return [shared_allies, shared_rivals, complementarity, same_state, balance, deg_product]

2.5.4 Cross-Validation and Model Comparison

To test whether the additional features actually improve prediction, we compare four models using 5-fold edge-holdout cross-validation. In each fold, we randomly remove roughly 20% of the alliance edges, fit each model on the remaining graph, score all held-out edges and an equal number of randomly sampled non-edges, and compute the AUC-ROC. The four models are:

  1. Jaccard coefficient alone (baseline, no fitting)
  2. Adamic-Adar index alone (baseline, no fitting)
  3. Logistic regression with topology features only (SharedAllies + DegreeProduct)
  4. Logistic regression with all 6 features (topology + specialty + rivalry + geography + balance)

If the full model’s AUC exceeds the topology-only model, the non-topological features carry predictive signal. If both logistic models exceed Jaccard and Adamic-Adar, the parametric framework adds value beyond raw similarity scores.

It is reasonable as a first approximation: geographic proximity, specialty complementarity, and shared enemies do not disappear when a hub falls. 2.6 Topic 4: Network Resilience — Targeted Node Removal

A fundamental question in network science is: how does a network respond when a critical node is removed? This is known as network robustness analysis or attack tolerance (Albert et al., 2000). In the cartel context, node removal simulates real-world disruption events — the arrest, death, or incapacitation of a cartel leader and the consequent dissolution of that organization’s alliances.

Algorithmic Framework

The simulation is based on two classical network attack strategies from the robustness literature:

Algorithm 1: Single Targeted Node Removal

Given a graph \(G = (V, E)\) and a target node \(v^* \in V\):

  1. Snapshot pre-removal metrics on \(G\): compute \(N\), \(E\), density \(\rho = \frac{2E}{N(N-1)}\), global clustering coefficient \(C\), number of connected components \(k\), size of the largest connected component (LCC), diameter \(d\) and average shortest path length \(\bar{\ell}\) of the LCC.
  2. Remove \(v^*\) and all its incident edges: \(G' = G - \{v^*\}\), which deletes all edges \(\{(v^*, u) : u \in \mathcal{N}(v^*)\}\) where \(\mathcal{N}(v^*)\) is the neighborhood of \(v^*\).
  3. Prune new isolates: any node \(u\) whose degree becomes 0 after removal (i.e., \(u\) was connected to the rest of the network only through \(v^*\)) is also removed, since isolated nodes are not meaningful for structural analysis.
  4. Recompute all metrics on \(G'\) and report the difference \(\Delta\).

The choice of which node to remove is guided by centrality. We target the node with the highest degree centrality \(C_D(v) = \frac{\deg(v)}{N-1}\) — in this case CJNG (El Mencho), which is also the highest-betweenness node.

Algorithm 2: Sequential Targeted Attack (Iterative Hub Removal)

This extends Algorithm 1 to a sequence of \(k\) removals and is the more realistic model of sustained law enforcement operations:

  1. Set \(G_0 = G\), \(t = 0\).
  2. While \(t < k\) and \(G_t\) is non-empty:
    • Compute degree of all nodes in \(G_t\).
    • Select \(v_t^* = \arg\max_{v \in V_t} \deg_t(v)\) (the current highest-degree node).
    • Remove \(v_t^*\): \(G_{t+1} = G_t - \{v_t^*\}\), prune new isolates.
    • Record \(|LCC_{t+1}|\), number of components, and edges remaining.
    • \(t \leftarrow t + 1\).
  3. Plot the fragmentation curve: \(|LCC_t|\) as a function of \(t\).

Crucially, the degree is recalculated after each removal. This is an adaptive attack — the next target depends on the current graph state, not the original ranking. This is more realistic than removing all top-\(k\) nodes simultaneously, because law enforcement operations unfold sequentially and the network reorganizes between each disruption.

Why Scale-Free-Like Networks Are Especially Vulnerable

Albert et al. (2000) proved a fundamental asymmetry:

  • Random failure: removing nodes uniformly at random has minimal impact on network connectivity, because most removed nodes are low-degree peripheral nodes.
  • Targeted attack: removing the highest-degree node first causes maximum disruption per removal, because the hub’s many edges are all severed simultaneously.

The mathematical intuition comes from the percolation threshold. In a network with degree distribution \(P(k)\), the critical fraction of nodes that must be removed to destroy the giant component is:

\[f_c = 1 - \frac{1}{\kappa - 1}, \quad \text{where} \quad \kappa = \frac{\langle k^2 \rangle}{\langle k \rangle}\]

For heavy-tailed degree distributions, \(\kappa\) is large (driven by high-degree hubs), so \(f_c \to 1\) under random failure (very robust). But under targeted attack, each removal reduces \(\kappa\) substantially, leading to rapid collapse.

Assumptions

  1. Node ≈ Organization: We equate removing a node with neutralizing the entire cartel organization it represents. In reality, killing a leader (El Mencho) does not instantly dissolve CJNG — but it does sever the decision-making center that maintained alliances, making this a reasonable first-order approximation.
  2. Alliance-only graph: We perform the simulation on the positive-ties subgraph only. Rivalry edges are excluded because we are interested in how the cooperation structure fragments. In practice, rivalry ties might actually intensify after a hub removal (rival groups may attack the weakened remnants).
  3. Static removal: We assume no rewiring — once CJNG is removed, its former allies do not immediately form new alliances with each other. This is a conservative (worst-case) assumption; in reality, some re-alignment would occur over time.
  4. Isolate pruning: Nodes that become completely disconnected after the removal are pruned from analysis. These represent cartels that had no other alliances besides the removed node — they effectively exit the cooperation network.
  5. Adaptive sequential attack: In the sequential removal, we re-rank by degree after each step rather than using the original ranking. This models a realistic strategy where law enforcement adapts to the evolving network.
  6. Undirected, unweighted edges for topology: While the original data has alliance weights (indicating alliance strength across states), the topological algorithms (degree, betweenness, clustering, path length) treat all edges as unweighted. This focuses the analysis on structural position rather than tie strength.

2.7 Optimal Targeting Under Rewiring

The Problem

The resilience analysis in Section 2.6 and the rewiring simulation in Section 2.5.5 treat node selection and post-disruption reorganization as separate steps. But if the goal is to choose which organizations to target, the two must be considered jointly: the optimal target set depends on how the network reorganizes afterward.

We formalize this as a combinatorial optimization problem. Let \(S \subseteq V\) be a set of nodes removed simultaneously, with \(|S| \leq k\) where \(k\) is the operational budget — the number of organizations that law enforcement can neutralize in a single coordinated operation. After removal, surviving organizations form new alliances according to the logistic model from Section 2.5.3. The objective is to choose \(S\) to minimize the largest connected component in the post-rewiring network:

\[S^* = \arg\min_{S \subseteq V,\; |S| = k} \; \text{LCC}\Big(\text{Rewire}\big(G \setminus S,\; \hat{P},\; \tau\big)\Big)\]

The function \(\text{Rewire}(G', \hat{P}, \tau)\) takes the surviving graph \(G' = G \setminus S\) (after pruning isolates), scores every non-adjacent pair with the logistic model, adds edges whose predicted probability exceeds the threshold \(\tau\), and returns the reorganized graph.

Threshold Calibration

The threshold \(\tau\) controls how aggressively the network rewires. We calibrate it through a parameter \(\alpha \in (0, 1)\) that represents the rewiring capacity — the fraction of lost edges that the surviving network can replace. For a given removal set \(S\):

  1. Count the edges lost: \(E_{\text{lost}} = |E(G)| - |E(G \setminus S)|\)
  2. On the surviving graph, score all non-edges with the logistic model
  3. Set \(\tau\) so that the number of non-edges exceeding \(\tau\) equals \(\alpha \cdot E_{\text{lost}}\)

We run the full analysis at \(\alpha = 0.25, 0.50, 0.75\) to show sensitivity. Low \(\alpha\) represents a hostile enforcement environment where new alliances are hard to form; high \(\alpha\) represents a permissive one.

Exhaustive Search

For \(k = 1\), there are 91 candidate removals. Each evaluation involves scoring \(\sim N^2/2\) non-edges with the logistic model, which takes milliseconds. We solve the problem exactly by exhaustive enumeration.

Baselines

We compare the optimal target set against four baselines:

  • Degree-based: remove the top-\(k\) nodes by degree centrality
  • Betweenness-based: remove the top-\(k\) nodes by betweenness centrality
  • Degree + rewiring: same targets as degree-based, but allow rewiring
  • Random: average post-rewiring LCC over 200 random \(k\)-subsets

The comparison between “degree + rewiring” and “optimal” shows the cost of ignoring rewiring when choosing targets.

Exhaustive search for optimal targeting under rewiring (k=1,2,3) 
from itertools import combinations

def rewire_and_measure(G_orig, removal_set, lr_model, G_riv, G_sign,
                       alpha, feature_func):
    """Remove nodes, rewire, return post-rewiring LCC size."""
    # Build subgraph without copies — use subgraph view then materialize only edges
    surviving = set(G_orig.nodes()) - removal_set
    G_rem = G_orig.subgraph(surviving).copy()

    # Prune isolates
    iso = [n for n in G_rem.nodes() if G_rem.degree(n) == 0]
    G_rem.remove_nodes_from(iso)

    if G_rem.number_of_nodes() == 0:
        return 0

    # Count lost edges
    e_lost = G_orig.number_of_edges() - G_rem.number_of_edges()
    n_new_edges = max(1, int(alpha * e_lost))

    # Score all non-edges — build feature matrix in bulk
    rem_nodes = sorted(G_rem.nodes())
    edge_set = set(G_rem.edges())
    non_edges = []
    for i, u in enumerate(rem_nodes):
        for v in rem_nodes[i+1:]:
            if (u, v) not in edge_set and (v, u) not in edge_set:
                non_edges.append((u, v))

    if len(non_edges) == 0:
        comps = list(nx.connected_components(G_rem))
        return max(len(c) for c in comps) if comps else 0

    # Precompute neighbor sets for speed
    all_neighbors = {n: set(G_rem.neighbors(n)) for n in rem_nodes}
    riv_neighbors = {n: set(G_riv.neighbors(n)) if G_riv.has_node(n) else set()
                     for n in rem_nodes}
    sign_neighbors = {n: set(G_sign.neighbors(n)) if G_sign.has_node(n) else set()
                      for n in rem_nodes}

    rows = []
    for u, v in non_edges:
        nu, nv = all_neighbors[u], all_neighbors[v]
        shared_allies = len(nu & nv)
        shared_rivals = len(riv_neighbors[u] & riv_neighbors[v])

        su = node_specialty.get(u, "Undocumented")
        sv = node_specialty.get(v, "Undocumented")
        comp = int(su != sv and su != "Undocumented" and sv != "Undocumented")
        same_st = int(node_state.get(u, "") == node_state.get(v, "")
                      and node_state.get(u, "") != "")

        balance = 0
        for w in sign_neighbors[u] & sign_neighbors[v]:
            wt_uw = G_sign[u][w].get("weight", 0)
            wt_vw = G_sign[v][w].get("weight", 0)
            balance += 1 if wt_uw * wt_vw > 0 else -1

        deg_prod = G_rem.degree(u) * G_rem.degree(v)
        rows.append([shared_allies, shared_rivals, comp, same_st, balance, deg_prod])

    X_re = np.array(rows)
    probs = lr_model.predict_proba(X_re)[:, 1]

    # Add top n_new_edges by probability
    top_idx = np.argsort(probs)[::-1][:n_new_edges]
    for idx in top_idx:
        u, v = non_edges[idx]
        G_rem.add_edge(u, v)

    comps = list(nx.connected_components(G_rem))
    return max(len(c) for c in comps) if comps else 0

3.3 Organizational Profiles and Network Position

The 150 organizations in the dataset are not a homogeneous set. Most are classified as Local (small territorial groups) or Cells (sub-units operating under a parent cartel). Only a handful qualify as National or Regional Cartels. Drug trafficking is the most common primary specialty, but a nontrivial number of organizations specialize in extortion, fuel theft (huachicoleo), or drug retail — activities that do not require the cross-border logistics of wholesale trafficking. Among organizations with a documented drug portfolio, methamphetamine and fentanyl appear most frequently, which tracks the DEA’s assessments (U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration, 2024, 2025) of the primary drug threats entering the US.

Geographically, organizations concentrate in a handful of states (Jalisco, Michoacán, Guerrero, Tamaulipas, Sinaloa). The alliance edges split roughly evenly between same-state and cross-state ties. Same-state alliances reflect territorial cooperation — groups sharing control of a region. Cross-state alliances reflect supply-chain logic — a production hub in one state allied with a distribution node in another. This mix matters because it means the network is not purely geographic; it has a logistic backbone that connects distant nodes.

3.4 Signed Network Visualization

The signed network visualization (Figure 5) uses a Fruchterman–Reingold force-directed layout, with nodes colored by organization type. Green edges represent alliances and red dashed edges represent rivalries. Node size is proportional to total degree in the signed graph.

Several patterns are visible in the layout. National cartels (red) sit near the center, pulling in a corona of smaller groups. Many local cells and armed wings are connected to the rest of the network through a single edge to one of these national hubs — remove that hub and they become isolates. The rivalry edges (red, dashed) tend to run between clusters rather than within them: groups that are allied with each other rarely have rivalries between them, while groups in different alliance clusters frequently do. This pattern aligns with structural balance theory (Cartwright & Harary, 1956) — the network approximates a partition into two or more antagonistic blocs where within-bloc ties are positive and between-bloc ties are negative.

The layout also makes visible how CJNG’s position differs from Sinaloa’s. CJNG connects to allies scattered across the graph, reaching into multiple geographic clusters. Sinaloa’s allies are more geographically concentrated. This difference in alliance topology foreshadows the different consequences of disrupting each hub, which we examine in Section 3.8.

3.6 Degree Distribution Evidence

The log-log degree distribution plot (Figure 6) compares the three networks. The ER graph produces a tight Poisson-like cluster where most nodes have similar degree — no hubs, no long tail. The BA graph produces the expected power-law slope. The real network falls somewhere between: it has a long right tail (CJNG at degree 28, Sinaloa at 21, then a sharp drop to 6) that the ER model cannot produce, but the tail is not smooth enough to fit a clean power law. Most nodes have degree 1 or 2; a handful have degree 4–6; and then CJNG and Sinaloa sit far to the right as outliers.

With only 91 nodes, we cannot make a rigorous power-law claim — Clauset et al. (2009) show that reliable power-law identification requires larger samples. What we can say is that the degree distribution is heavy-tailed relative to ER, that the network is hub-dominated, and that the two largest hubs account for a disproportionate share of all edges. This degree heterogeneity is what makes the network vulnerable to targeted attack, as we show in Section 3.8.

The diameter drops from 8 to 3 and average path length from 3.30 to 2.14. The side-by-side visualization (Figure 9) shows this plainly. The left panel has CJNG (red) at the center with spokes radiating outward to its allies (orange). The right panel shows those allies either gone or pushed to the periphery, with a smaller, denser residual network.

4. Conclusion

4.1 Answers to Research Questions

RQ1: The cartel alliance network has 91 non-isolate organizations connected by 91 alliance edges across 8 components. The structure is sparse (\(\rho = 0.022\)) and heavily concentrated around two national cartels — CJNG and Sinaloa — which together account for a disproportionate share of all edges. Organizational type tracks network position: national cartels hold hub and broker roles, while local groups and cells sit at the periphery, often connected through a single tie to a national hub.

RQ2: The alliance network does not match either null model. It clusters more than Erdős–Rényi (which produces zero triangles at this density) and is more fragmented than Barabási–Albert (which produces a single connected component). Both null models also produce longer average path lengths. The network’s combination of high clustering, short paths, and multi-component fragmentation points to mechanisms — geographic proximity, triadic closure through trust, shared rivalry pressure — that random wiring cannot replicate.

RQ3: Topological link prediction (Jaccard, Adamic-Adar) identifies 15 pairs with identical alliance neighborhoods that lack a direct tie. The dyadic utility logistic regression extends this by incorporating rivalry structure, specialty complementarity, geographic proximity, and structural balance alongside topology. Cross-validation shows whether these non-topological features improve prediction over topology alone. The logistic coefficients reveal what drives alliance formation: shared allies (triadic closure), shared enemies (balance theory), and complementary specialties (supply-chain logic) each carry interpretable weight.

RQ4: Removing CJNG eliminates 28 edges (31% of the total), turns 23 organizations into isolates, and cuts the LCC from 50 to 26. But the network does not stay fragmented. The post-disruption rewiring simulation predicts which new alliances form in the surviving network, addressing whether hub removal dismantles the network or merely triggers reorganization.

RQ5: The optimal targeting analysis solves the problem exactly for \(k = 1\) across three rewiring capacity levels. The results show whether the naive strategy (target the highest-degree nodes) remains optimal once rewiring is accounted for, or whether less obvious targets — brokers, geographic bridges — produce more durable fragmentation by leaving surviving organizations without the conditions (shared state, shared enemies) needed to form new alliances.

4.2 Substantive Interpretation

Two organizations — CJNG and Sinaloa — hold the alliance network together. Between them they are allied with roughly half the non-isolate nodes. This concentration of connectivity around two rival hubs creates a bipolar structure: most smaller organizations are satellites of one hub or the other, and the rivalry between the two hubs (weight = 7, the maximum in the dataset) defines the principal axis of conflict.

The brokerage analysis complicates this picture. Several local organizations (Talibanes, Unión Tepito, El Abuelo, Viagras) rank high on betweenness despite having few alliances. They occupy bottleneck positions connecting subgraphs that have no alternative path between them. Disrupting these brokers would fragment communication channels in ways that raw degree counts do not predict — a point made by Duijn et al. (2014) in the context of Dutch criminal networks.

The specialty data shows that the alliance network connects organizations with different criminal portfolios. National drug trafficking cartels ally with local extortion groups, toll-collectors, and cells. This looks more like functional complementarity (allying with organizations that offer different capabilities) than homophily (allying with similar organizations). The geographic analysis reinforces this: cross-state alliances link production regions to distribution corridors, suggesting that the alliance network partly maps supply-chain relationships.

The temporal crime trends frame the stakes. The BACRIM 2020 snapshot was taken during a period of nearly 37,000 annual homicides, up sharply from 2017. That escalation coincides with the fragmentation of major cartels under the kingpin strategy — the same kind of hub removal our simulation models. Calderón et al. (2015) documented the mechanism: decapitation creates power vacuums that produce violence as factions compete for control. Our node-removal results quantify the structural damage, but they do not capture the violence that fills the vacuum. Fragmentation is not the same as pacification.

4.3 Limitations

The main limitation is temporal. The 2020 snapshot captures alliances and rivalries at a single point in time. We cannot observe how ties form, dissolve, or shift in response to arrests, killings, or territorial changes. A longitudinal dataset would allow testing whether link predictions materialize and whether the fragmentation dynamics we simulate actually play out as modeled.

The logistic link prediction model incorporates node attributes (specialty, geography) and signed network features (shared rivals, balance), but it still uses a static cross-section. Temporal features — alliance duration, recent enforcement actions, evolving territorial control — are unavailable in the 2020 snapshot and would likely improve prediction if longitudinal data existed.

The data is necessarily incomplete. Covert relationships may go unrecorded, and the boundary of who counts as a distinct organization is sometimes blurry. Sparrow (1991) identified incompleteness and fuzzy boundaries as fundamental challenges of criminal network analysis, and they apply here.

We treat each organization as a unitary actor, but internal factions (Los Chapitos vs. Los Mayos within Sinaloa, for instance) may have distinct and even conflicting alliance structures. The 2024 Sinaloa civil war demonstrates this limitation in practice.

Finally, our algorithms treat all alliance edges as equal. The original data includes alliance weights (number of states where the alliance is active), which could proxy for tie strength. A weighted analysis might produce different centrality rankings and different link predictions.

4.4 Future Work

Temporal network analysis over multiple BACRIM snapshots would allow tracking of alliance evolution and direct testing of both the topological and logistic link predictions. The logistic model could be extended with temporal covariates (time since last enforcement action, alliance age) if longitudinal data became available. Signed-link prediction — predicting both future alliances and future rivalries from the same framework — is a natural extension. ERGMs or STERGMs could test the same hypotheses within a formal statistical network framework that accounts for dyadic dependencies. The optimal targeting framework could be extended to sequential (multi-round) operations where the network rewires between each targeting step, and to multi-objective formulations that trade off fragmentation against predicted violence intensity. Agent-based simulation of post-disruption rewiring, where organizations make sequential alliance decisions rather than simultaneous ones, would add dynamic realism to the static rewiring prediction.

4.5 Connection to Recent Events (2024–2026)

The BACRIM 2020 dataset is a static snapshot, but events since its publication track the structural vulnerabilities we identified with uncomfortable precision.

The Fall of the Sinaloa Cartel’s Leadership

Ismael “El Mayo” Zambada, co-founder of the Sinaloa Cartel, was arrested in Texas on July 25, 2024, reportedly lured to the US by Joaquín Guzmán López (son of El Chapo). In August 2025, Zambada pled guilty to narcotics and money laundering charges in a Brooklyn federal court. In the 2020 network, the Sinaloa Cartel (node N10013) has the second-highest degree centrality. Removing its top leader amounts to a targeted attack on one of the two dominant hubs.

The arrest triggered a civil war within the Sinaloa Cartel between “Los Chapitos” (the Guzmán sons’ faction, which orchestrated the abduction) and Zambada loyalists (“Los Mayos”). In network terms, one high-degree node splits into two or more competing lower-degree nodes. Alliance edges may be replaced by rivalry edges as former collaborators take sides. The organizational unity that gave Sinaloa its hub status no longer holds.

The Killing of “El Mencho” and CJNG’s Crisis

On February 22, 2026, Mexican military forces killed Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes (“El Mencho”), founder and leader of CJNG, in Jalisco. In the 2020 network, CJNG is the highest-degree and highest-betweenness node — the most connected hub and the most critical bridge. El Mencho’s elimination is the real-world analog of the single-node removal simulation in Section 2.6.

Our simulation predicts that removing CJNG drops the LCC from 50 to 26 and turns 23 satellite organizations into isolates. Within hours of El Mencho’s death, suspected CJNG operatives launched revenge attacks in Jalisco (vehicle bombs, transit blockades), and the US Consulate in Guadalajara issued a shelter-in-place order. Whether CJNG’s alliance network actually fragmented as the model predicts is an empirical question that will become clearer over the coming months, but the immediate destabilization is consistent with hub removal in a centralized network.

US Designation as Terrorist Organizations (February 2025)

In February 2025, the United States designated both CJNG and the Sinaloa Cartel as Foreign Terrorist Organizations (FTOs). These are the two dominant hubs in the 2020 network, and they share a rivalry tie with weight 7 — the maximum in the dataset, indicating antagonism across 7 Mexican states.

The terrorist designation triggered mass extraditions: 29 cartel figures in February 2025 and another 26 in August 2025. This amounts to a systematic strategy of targeting high-centrality nodes and their immediate neighbors — the approach that Duijn et al. (2014) found most effective in their analysis of criminal network disruption.

Validation of Alliance-Network Structure

The dismantling of Los Cuinis — CJNG’s most important ally (alliance weight = 6 in the dataset) — fits the pattern. Los Cuinis leader Abigael González Valencia was extradited in August 2025 and sentenced to 30 years. Removing a hub’s strongest alliance tie before removing the hub itself weakens the hub’s position and reduces the network’s capacity to reorganize after the hub falls.

In February 2026, a $10 million bounty was placed on Sinaloa Cartel leaders operating in Baja California. The Sinaloa civil war continued to produce hundreds of casualties. The factions created by Zambada’s arrest remain in active conflict — the network did not heal.

# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Context and Motivation

Organized crime operates as a relational system where alliances and rivalries shape power, coordination, and conflict among criminal organizations. In Mexico, drug cartels form complex webs of cooperation and competition that determine territorial control, operational capacity, and the dynamics of violence. Between 2012 and 2021, Mexico recorded over 290,000 homicides and over 175,000 missing persons — figures that correlate closely with cartel fragmentation and inter-organizational conflict [@inegi2022].

The BACRIM 2020 dataset [@prietocuriel2023bacrim] represents a signed network capturing both positive ties (alliances) and negative ties (rivalries) among 150 Mexican criminal organizations as of 2020. Unlike many criminal network datasets that focus on individual actors, BACRIM operates at the organizational level, treating each cartel, cell, or local group as a node. This allows us to study the macro-structure of criminal cooperation - who allies with whom, who competes against whom, and which organizations serve as bridges or brokers.

Network science provides a natural framework for this analysis. Rather than treating each cartel as an isolated actor, we model the full relational structure as a graph $G = (V, E)$, where vertices $V$ represent organizations and edges $E$ represent observed alliances or rivalries. This formalism captures dependencies, brokerage, clustering, and potential future link formation - dimensions that are invisible to traditional organizational charts or case-by-case intelligence analysis [@sparrow1991application].

We enrich the network data with organizational specialties - the primary criminal activities (drug trafficking, extortion, fuel theft, kidnapping) and drug portfolios (fentanyl, methamphetamine, cocaine, heroin) of each cartel - drawn from DEA National Drug Threat Assessments [@dea2024; @dea2025], Congressional Research Service reports, and InSight Crime profiles. This allows us to test whether alliance formation is driven by functional complementarity (cartels with different specialties allying to form integrated supply chains) or homophily (cartels with similar activities preferring to cooperate).

Events between 2024 and 2026 — the arrest of Ismael "El Mayo" Zambada, the killing of CJNG leader "El Mencho," and the US designation of both cartels as terrorist organizations — track the structural vulnerabilities identified from this 2020 snapshot with notable precision. We discuss these connections in [Section 4.5](#connection-to-recent-events-20242026).

## 1.2 Research Questions

The research questions we are trying to answer in this document are:

- What are the key structural properties of the cartel alliance network, and how do organizational characteristics (type, specialty, drug portfolio) relate to network position?
- Is the observed alliance network compatible with a random process, or does it show structured organization?
- Which currently disconnected cartel pairs are most likely to form future alliances, and do non-topological features (specialty, rivalry, geography) improve prediction over topology alone?
- How does the network structure change when a critical hub node is removed, and how does the surviving network plausibly reorganize?
- Given a limited budget of $k$ simultaneous removals, which target set minimizes the largest connected component after the network rewires?

## 1.3 Core Analytical Objectives

Beyond the descriptive and comparative analyses above, this report focuses on two main investigative objectives:

1. Finding Hidden Connections in the Network (Link Prediction): Not all relationships between criminal organizations are directly observable. Two cartels that are not currently allied may nonetheless share many mutual allies, operate in adjacent territories, or occupy structurally similar positions in the network. Link prediction algorithms exploit these patterns to identify the most plausible hidden or future connections - pairs of organizations that do not yet have a direct alliance but are topologically primed to form one.

We use two complementary approaches: the Jaccard coefficient, which measures the fraction of shared neighbors between two nodes, and the Adamic-Adar index, which weights shared neighbors by their exclusivity (rare shared connections are more informative than common ones). Together, these methods reveal the "invisible wiring" of the cartel ecosystem - alliances that likely exist but are unrecorded, or that are likely to emerge in the near future.

2. Network Resilience: What Happens When a Critical Node Is Removed? Criminal networks, like many real-world networks, are heterogeneous - a small number of highly connected hubs hold together a large periphery of smaller organizations. This raises a critical strategic questions: what happens if a hub is eliminated? Does the network fragment into isolated clusters, or does it remain largely intact?

We simulate the targeted removal of the most central node (CJNG / El Mencho) and measure the resulting structural disruption: changes in connectivity, fragmentation into components, increased path lengths, and loss of cohesion. We extend this to a sequential attack on the top-5 hubs to trace the full fragmentation curve. This analysis has direct real-world relevance: the February 2026 killing of El Mencho and the 2024 arrest of El Mayo Zambada are precisely the kind of hub removals our simulation models, allowing us to validate network-theoretic predictions against observed outcomes.

## 1.5 Literature Review

The application of network science to organized crime has grown substantially over the past two decades. Below we review key contributions that inform our analytical framework.

### Criminal Networks as Complex Systems

@morselli2009inside established the foundational framework for studying criminal organizations as networks in *Inside Criminal Networks*. He showed that criminal groups exhibit structural properties — degree heterogeneity, clustering, and brokerage — that reflect strategic coordination rather than randomness. Centrality measures (degree and betweenness) can identify leaders and brokers in covert networks, even when intelligence is incomplete.

@sparrow1991application was among the first to argue that social network analysis provides law enforcement with tools superior to hierarchy-based organizational charts. He identified three challenges of criminal network analysis that remain relevant: incompleteness (missing nodes and edges), fuzzy boundaries (unclear group membership), and dynamics (networks evolve over time).

### Signed Networks and Structural Balance

Our dataset contains both alliance (+) and rivalry (−) ties, making it a signed network. The theoretical basis for analyzing such structures comes from @heider1946attitudes's balance theory and its graph-theoretic formalization by @cartwright1956structural: in a balanced network, "the enemy of my enemy is my friend" and "the friend of my friend is my friend." @leskovec2010predicting extended balance theory to large-scale online networks and found that real signed networks tend toward structural balance — a pattern relevant to interpreting cartel alliance and rivalry structures.

### Network Robustness and Targeted Attacks

@albert2000error demonstrated that scale-free networks (those with heavy-tailed degree distributions) are robust to random node failures but highly vulnerable to targeted attacks on hubs. Arresting random low-level operatives has minimal impact on network connectivity; targeting the most connected nodes causes disproportionate fragmentation. @callaway2000network provided the complementary percolation-theory framework for understanding these phase transitions in network connectivity.

@duijn2014relative applied these robustness ideas to criminal networks specifically, finding that targeting brokers (high betweenness) is often more effective than targeting hubs (high degree), because broker removal fragments communication channels even when fewer edges are lost.

### Link Prediction in Criminal Networks

@libennowell2007link established the theoretical framework for topological link prediction, comparing common neighbors, Jaccard coefficient, and Adamic-Adar index. They showed that network topology alone — without external features — can predict future edges with significant accuracy.

@berlusconi2016link applied link prediction to Italian Mafia co-offending networks and found that Jaccard and Adamic-Adar metrics identified hidden connections that intelligence agencies later confirmed. Shared allies create structural pressure toward alliance formation.

They documented 150 organizations across Mexican states and showed that the network exhibits community structure aligned with geographic regions.

## 2.3 Topic 1: Foundations of Networks

We compute fundamental network metrics on the **alliance graph**. These metrics characterize the global topology (how dense, how far apart are nodes, how clustered are connections) and identify the most important individual actors.

### How Do We Determine the Importance of a Node?

In network science, "importance" is not a single concept — different centrality measures capture different dimensions of a node's structural role. We use two complementary measures:

#### Degree Centrality — Who Has the Most Connections?

*Intuition.* The simplest way to measure a node's importance is to count how many connections it has. A cartel allied with 20 others is more "important" (in the sense of reach and coordination capacity) than one allied with 2.

*Formal definition.* For a node $v$ in an undirected graph $G = (V, E)$ with $N = |V|$ nodes, the degree $k_v$ is the number of edges incident to $v$:

$$k_v = \sum_{j \in V} A_{vj}$$

where $A$ is the adjacency matrix ($A_{vj} = 1$ if $(v,j) \in E$, else 0). The degree centrality normalizes this by the maximum possible connections:

$$C_D(v) = \frac{k_v}{N - 1}$$

The value ranges from 0 (isolated node) to 1 (connected to every other node).

*In the cartel context:* a high degree centrality means the organization maintains alliances with many other cartels simultaneously. This makes it a hub — a central coordinator in the cooperation network. CJNG, for example, has the highest degree centrality because it is allied with more organizations than any other cartel. Removing a high-degree node severs many connections at once, which is why law enforcement targets these hubs.

*Algorithmic complexity:* Computing degree centrality for all nodes is $O(N + E)$ — simply count the edges per node and normalize.

#### Betweenness Centrality — Who Controls the Flow?

*Intuition.* Even a node with few connections can be critically important if it lies on the *only path* between two otherwise separate groups. Think of a small cartel that is the sole intermediary between a production hub and a distribution network — removing it disconnects the entire supply chain.

*Formal definition.* Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on the shortest paths between other pairs of nodes. For a node $v$:

$$C_B(v) = \sum_{\substack{s \neq v \neq t \\ s, t \in V}} \frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}$$

where $\sigma_{st}$ is the total number of shortest paths from node $s$ to node $t$, and $\sigma_{st}(v)$ is the number of those shortest paths that pass through $v$. In NetworkX, this value is normalized by dividing by $\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}$ for undirected graphs, so it falls in $[0, 1]$.

*In the cartel context:* a high betweenness centrality identifies brokers or bridge organizations — cartels that connect otherwise separate clusters. Even if a cartel has relatively few direct alliances (moderate degree), it can have high betweenness if it is the *only path* linking two groups of cartels. Removing such a broker fragments the network into disconnected components.

*Algorithm: Brandes' method.* Computing betweenness centrality naively requires enumerating all shortest paths — $O(N^3)$ for unweighted graphs. @brandes2001faster developed an efficient algorithm that runs in $O(NE)$ time:

1. For each source node $s$, perform a BFS to compute shortest-path distances and counts $\sigma_{st}$ for all targets $t$.
2. In a single backward pass (processing nodes in reverse BFS order), accumulate a dependency score $\delta_s(v) = \sum_{t: v \text{ is on } s{\text{-}}t \text{ path}} \frac{\sigma_{sv}}{\sigma_{st}} (1 + \delta_s(t))$.
3. Sum the dependency scores: $C_B(v) = \sum_s \delta_s(v)$.

This avoids explicitly enumerating all $O(N^2)$ shortest paths and instead propagates dependency information through the BFS tree.

#### Why Use Both Measures Together?

| Scenario | Degree | Betweenness | Interpretation |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| High degree, high betweenness | High | High | Dominant hub — many allies AND a bridge (e.g., CJNG) |
| High degree, low betweenness | High | Low | Local hub — many allies but within a dense cluster; redundant paths exist around it |
| Low degree, high betweenness | Low | High | Critical bridge — few allies but connects otherwise separated groups; removal causes fragmentation |
| Low degree, low betweenness | Low | Low | Peripheral node — few allies, easily bypassed; removal has minimal impact |

A node that ranks high on both measures (like CJNG in this network) is the most strategically important target: it is both highly connected and structurally irreplaceable. Conversely, a node that ranks high on betweenness but not degree represents a hidden vulnerability — its removal would fragment the network despite its seemingly modest alliance portfolio.

This dual-centrality framework directly informs our targeted node removal simulation in [Section 2.6](#topic-4-network-resilience--targeted-node-removal): we select CJNG as the removal target precisely because it dominates both rankings.

The density $\rho = \frac{2E}{N(N-1)}$ measures the fraction of all possible edges that actually exist. A low density indicates that alliances are selective — organizations do not ally indiscriminately but rather form targeted partnerships.

The global clustering coefficient $C = \frac{3 \times \text{triangles}}{\text{connected triples}}$ measures how often two allies of the same cartel are also allies of each other (triadic closure). A high clustering coefficient relative to a random graph of the same size suggests that "friends of friends tend to be friends" — a pattern consistent with trust-based alliance formation.

The diameter $d = \max_{u,v \in \text{LCC}} d(u,v)$ is the longest shortest path in the largest connected component, and the average path length $\bar{\ell}$ measures how many alliance hops are needed, on average, to connect any two cartels. Small values relative to $N$ suggest a "small-world" property — even in a sparse network, coordination can propagate quickly.

## 2.4 Topic 2: Random Graph Models

A central question in network analysis is whether the observed structure could have arisen by chance. To answer this, we compare the empirical alliance network against two null models — theoretical random graph constructions that serve as baselines.

### The Erdős–Rényi Model $G(N, p)$

*Intuition.* The simplest random graph: take $N$ nodes and independently connect each pair with probability $p$. Every possible edge is equally likely, and there is no preferential attachment, clustering, or community structure by construction.

*Formal definition [@erdos1959random].* Given $N$ nodes, each of the $\binom{N}{2}$ possible edges exists independently with probability $p$. The expected number of edges is $\mathbb{E}[E] = \binom{N}{2} p$ and the expected degree of each node is:

$$\langle k \rangle = (N-1)p$$

The degree distribution follows a Poisson distribution for large $N$:

$$P(k) \approx \frac{\langle k \rangle^k}{k!} e^{-\langle k \rangle}$$

This means all nodes have roughly similar degree — there are no "hubs." The clustering coefficient converges to $C_{\text{ER}} = p = \frac{\langle k \rangle}{N-1}$, which is typically very small for sparse networks.

*Why is ER a useful null model?* It represents the hypothesis that alliances form *at random* with no structural logic. If the real network has significantly higher clustering or different degree distribution than ER, we can reject this hypothesis and conclude that alliances are governed by some organizing principle (trust, geography, complementary specialization).

*To match our data*, we set $p = \frac{2E_{\text{real}}}{N(N-1)}$ so that the ER graph has the same expected number of edges as the empirical alliance network.

### The Barabási–Albert Model (Preferential Attachment)

*Intuition.* In many real-world networks, new nodes do not connect randomly — they prefer to link to *already well-connected* nodes. This "rich get richer" mechanism, called preferential attachment, produces hub-dominated networks with heavy-tailed degree distributions.

*Formal definition [@barabasi1999emergence].* Start with $m_0$ connected nodes. At each time step, a new node arrives and connects to $m$ existing nodes with probability proportional to their current degree:

$$\Pi(k_i) = \frac{k_i}{\sum_j k_j}$$

A node with degree $k$ is $k$ times more likely to attract new connections than a node with degree 1. The resulting degree distribution follows a power law:

$$P(k) \sim k^{-\gamma}, \quad \gamma = 3$$

In log-log space, this appears as a straight line with slope $-3$. The clustering coefficient of BA graphs is higher than ER but still lower than most real-world networks.

*Why is BA a useful comparison?* If the real cartel network shows heavy-tailed degree distribution (a few hubs with many alliances, many groups with few), this is consistent with a preferential attachment mechanism: new or expanding cartels preferentially ally with already-powerful organizations, reinforcing their dominance.

### 2.4.3 Small-World Analysis

A network is said to exhibit the small-world property [@watts1998collective] if it has:

1. High clustering relative to a random graph: $C_{\text{real}} \gg C_{\text{ER}}$
2. Short path lengths comparable to a random graph: $L_{\text{real}} \approx L_{\text{ER}}$

The small-world coefficient $\sigma$ formalizes this:

$$\sigma = \frac{C_{\text{real}} / C_{\text{ER}}}{L_{\text{real}} / L_{\text{ER}}}$$

A value $\sigma > 1$ indicates small-world properties. Intuitively, a small-world network has dense local clusters (allies of allies tend to be allies) but also enough "shortcuts" (cross-cluster alliances) to keep all nodes close in terms of path length.

```{python}
#| label: small-world
#| code-summary: "Compute small-world coefficient"

C_real = nx.transitivity(G_alliance_connected)
C_er = nx.transitivity(G_er)

lcc_real = max(nx.connected_components(G_alliance_connected), key=len)
L_real = nx.average_shortest_path_length(
    G_alliance_connected.subgraph(lcc_real).copy())

lcc_er = max(nx.connected_components(G_er), key=len)
L_er = nx.average_shortest_path_length(G_er.subgraph(lcc_er).copy())

if C_er > 0 and L_er > 0:
    sigma = (C_real / C_er) / (L_real / L_er)
    print(f"Small-World Analysis:")
    print(f"  C_real = {C_real:.4f}, C_ER = {C_er:.4f} → ratio = {C_real/C_er:.2f}")
    print(f"  L_real = {L_real:.4f}, L_ER = {L_er:.4f} → ratio = {L_real/L_er:.2f}")
    print(f"  σ = {sigma:.2f}")
    print(f"  {'Small-world property detected (σ > 1)' if sigma > 1 else 'No clear small-world property (σ ≤ 1)'}")
else:
    print("Cannot compute small-world coefficient (ER clustering or path length is 0)")
```

## 2.5 Topic 3: Link Prediction

We predict future alliances among currently non-adjacent pairs in the alliance graph using two topological similarity metrics. The fundamental idea, formalized by @libennowell2007link, is that two nodes sharing many common neighbors are likely to form a direct connection in the future — the "friends of friends" principle.

### Algorithm: Jaccard Coefficient

*Intuition.* The Jaccard coefficient measures the *proportion* of neighbors that two nodes share, relative to their total combined neighborhood. It penalizes pairs where one node has many neighbors but shares few of them with the other.

*Formal definition.* For two non-adjacent nodes $u$ and $v$ with neighborhoods $\mathcal{N}(u)$ and $\mathcal{N}(v)$:

$$J(u, v) = \frac{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)|}{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cup \mathcal{N}(v)|}$$

The value ranges from 0 (no shared neighbors) to 1 (identical neighborhoods). In the cartel context, $J = 0.5$ between two organizations means that half of their combined alliance portfolio overlaps — a strong topological signal that a direct alliance is plausible.

*Computational complexity.* For a single pair, computing $J$ requires set intersection and union of neighbor lists: $O(\min(k_u, k_v))$ with sorted adjacency lists. For all non-edges: $O(N^2 \cdot \bar{k})$.

### Algorithm: Adamic-Adar Index

*Intuition.* Not all shared neighbors are equally informative. If two cartels share a common ally that itself has 50 alliances, this is less meaningful (the common ally allies with everyone) than if they share a common ally that has only 3 alliances (a selective, exclusive connection). Adamic-Adar weights each shared neighbor inversely by the logarithm of its degree.

*Formal definition [@adamic2003friends].* For two non-adjacent nodes $u$ and $v$:

$$AA(u, v) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)} \frac{1}{\log |\mathcal{N}(w)|}$$

Each shared neighbor $w$ contributes $\frac{1}{\log k_w}$ to the score. Low-degree shared neighbors contribute more because they represent selective, informative connections. A shared ally with degree 2 contributes $\frac{1}{\log 2} \approx 1.44$, while one with degree 100 contributes only $\frac{1}{\log 100} \approx 0.22$.

*Why Adamic-Adar complements Jaccard.* Jaccard treats all shared neighbors equally, while Adamic-Adar gives more weight to rare connections. In practice, the top-ranked predictions by both metrics are often similar, but Adamic-Adar can surface pairs connected through exclusive intermediaries that Jaccard would undervalue.

### 2.5.3 Dyadic Utility Model

Jaccard and Adamic-Adar use topology alone. They cannot distinguish between two pairs that share the same number of neighbors but differ in every other dimension — different specialties, different states, different positions in the rivalry network. A more complete model of alliance formation treats each potential pair as a decision: two organizations ally when the structural, functional, and strategic conditions are right.

We frame this as a logistic regression on dyads. For every pair $(i, j)$ among the 91 non-isolate nodes, the response is 1 if an alliance edge exists and 0 otherwise. The feature vector captures six dimensions of dyadic affinity:

| Feature | Definition | Rationale |
|:---|:---|:---|
| SharedAllies | Common neighbors in alliance graph | Triadic closure: mutual allies create trust |
| SharedRivals | Common enemies in signed graph | Balance theory: shared enemies push toward alliance |
| Complementarity | 1 if primary specialties differ, 0 otherwise | Supply-chain logic: different capabilities attract |
| SameState | 1 if both in the same Mexican state | Geographic proximity enables cooperation |
| BalanceScore | Sum over common signed neighbors: +1 if both ties have same sign, -1 otherwise | Heider balance in the signed network |
| DegreeProduct | $\deg(i) \times \deg(j)$ in alliance graph | Preferential attachment: hubs attract more ties |

The model is:

$$P(\text{alliance} \mid i, j) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_{ij})}}$$

With 91 edges among $\binom{91}{2} = 4{,}095$ possible pairs, the positive rate is 2.2%. We use class-weighted logistic regression (`class_weight='balanced'` in scikit-learn) to prevent the model from trivially predicting all zeros. Each coefficient has a direct interpretation: $\beta_k > 0$ means that feature $k$ increases the probability of alliance, and $\exp(\beta_k)$ gives the odds ratio.

```{python}
#| label: dyadic-features
#| code-summary: "Build dyadic feature matrix and fit logistic regression"

# --- Build rivalry-only subgraph (for SharedRivals) ---
G_rivalry = nx.Graph()
for u, v, d in G_signed.edges(data=True):
    if d.get("edge_type") == "rivalry":
        if u in G_alliance_connected and v in G_alliance_connected:
            G_rivalry.add_edge(u, v)

# --- All node pairs among the 91 non-isolate nodes ---
nodes_list = sorted(G_alliance_connected.nodes())
all_pairs = [(u, v) for i, u in enumerate(nodes_list) for v in nodes_list[i+1:]]

def compute_dyadic_features(u, v, G_all, G_riv, G_sign):
    """Compute 6 dyadic features for a node pair."""
    # SharedAllies
    nu = set(G_all.neighbors(u)) if G_all.has_node(u) else set()
    nv = set(G_all.neighbors(v)) if G_all.has_node(v) else set()
    shared_allies = len(nu & nv)

    # SharedRivals
    ru = set(G_riv.neighbors(u)) if G_riv.has_node(u) else set()
    rv = set(G_riv.neighbors(v)) if G_riv.has_node(v) else set()
    shared_rivals = len(ru & rv)

    # Complementarity (1 if different specialties, both documented)
    su = node_specialty.get(u, "Undocumented")
    sv = node_specialty.get(v, "Undocumented")
    complementarity = int(su != sv and su != "Undocumented" and sv != "Undocumented")

    # SameState
    same_state = int(node_state.get(u, "") == node_state.get(v, "")
                     and node_state.get(u, "") != "")

    # BalanceScore from signed graph
    balance = 0
    sign_neighbors_u = set(G_sign.neighbors(u)) if G_sign.has_node(u) else set()
    sign_neighbors_v = set(G_sign.neighbors(v)) if G_sign.has_node(v) else set()
    for w in sign_neighbors_u & sign_neighbors_v:
        wt_uw = G_sign[u][w].get("weight", 0)
        wt_vw = G_sign[v][w].get("weight", 0)
        balance += 1 if wt_uw * wt_vw > 0 else -1

    # DegreeProduct
    du = G_all.degree(u) if G_all.has_node(u) else 0
    dv = G_all.degree(v) if G_all.has_node(v) else 0
    deg_product = du * dv

    return [shared_allies, shared_rivals, complementarity, same_state, balance, deg_product]

# --- Build feature matrix ---
```

### 2.5.4 Cross-Validation and Model Comparison

To test whether the additional features actually improve prediction, we compare four models using 5-fold edge-holdout cross-validation. In each fold, we randomly remove roughly 20% of the alliance edges, fit each model on the remaining graph, score all held-out edges and an equal number of randomly sampled non-edges, and compute the AUC-ROC. The four models are:

1. Jaccard coefficient alone (baseline, no fitting)
2. Adamic-Adar index alone (baseline, no fitting)
3. Logistic regression with topology features only (SharedAllies + DegreeProduct)
4. Logistic regression with all 6 features (topology + specialty + rivalry + geography + balance)

If the full model's AUC exceeds the topology-only model, the non-topological features carry predictive signal. If both logistic models exceed Jaccard and Adamic-Adar, the parametric framework adds value beyond raw similarity scores.

```{python}
#| label: cross-validation
#| code-summary: "5-fold edge-holdout cross-validation comparing four link prediction models"

edges_list = list(G_alliance_connected.edges())
non_edge_set = set(all_pairs) - set((min(u,v), max(u,v))
                                     for u,v in G_alliance_connected.edges())
non_edge_list = list(non_edge_set)

np.random.seed(42)
edge_indices = np.random.permutation(len(edges_list))
fold_size = len(edges_list) // 5

auc_results = {"Jaccard": [], "Adamic_Adar": [],
               "Logistic_Topo": [], "Logistic_Full": []}

for fold in range(5):
    # Split edges
    test_idx = edge_indices[fold * fold_size : (fold + 1) * fold_size]
    train_idx = np.setdiff1d(edge_indices, test_idx)

    test_edges = [edges_list[i] for i in test_idx]
    train_edges = [edges_list[i] for i in train_idx]

    # Build training graph
    G_train = G_alliance_connected.copy()
    G_train.remove_edges_from(test_edges)

    # Sample negatives for test set (same count as held-out edges)
    neg_sample_idx = np.random.choice(len(non_edge_list),
                                       size=len(test_edges), replace=False)
    neg_test_pairs = [non_edge_list[i] for i in neg_sample_idx]

    test_pairs = test_edges + neg_test_pairs
    test_y = np.array([1] * len(test_edges) + [0] * len(neg_test_pairs))

    # --- Jaccard and Adamic-Adar scores on training graph ---
    jacc_scores = []
    aa_scores = []
    for u, v in test_pairs:
        if G_train.has_node(u) and G_train.has_node(v):
            cn = set(G_train.neighbors(u)) & set(G_train.neighbors(v))
            union = set(G_train.neighbors(u)) | set(G_train.neighbors(v))
            jacc_scores.append(len(cn) / len(union) if union else 0)
            aa_scores.append(sum(1 / np.log(G_train.degree(w))
                                 for w in cn if G_train.degree(w) > 1))
        else:
            jacc_scores.append(0)
            aa_scores.append(0)

    if len(set(test_y)) > 1:
        auc_results["Jaccard"].append(roc_auc_score(test_y, jacc_scores))
        auc_results["Adamic_Adar"].append(roc_auc_score(test_y, aa_scores))

    # --- Logistic models: recompute features on training graph ---
    X_train_fold = []
    y_train_fold = []
    for u, v in all_pairs:
        feats = compute_dyadic_features(u, v, G_train, G_rivalry, G_signed)
        X_train_fold.append(feats)
        y_train_fold.append(1 if G_train.has_edge(u, v) else 0)
    X_train_fold = np.array(X_train_fold)
    y_train_fold = np.array(y_train_fold)

    # Topo-only: SharedAllies (col 0) + DegreeProduct (col 5)
    lr_topo = LogisticRegression(class_weight='balanced', max_iter=1000,
                                  solver='lbfgs', random_state=42)
    lr_topo.fit(X_train_fold[:, [0, 5]], y_train_fold)

    lr_cv = LogisticRegression(class_weight='balanced', max_iter=1000,
                                solver='lbfgs', random_state=42)
    lr_cv.fit(X_train_fold, y_train_fold)

    # Score test pairs
    X_test = np.array([compute_dyadic_features(u, v, G_train, G_rivalry, G_signed)
                       for u, v in test_pairs])

    prob_topo = lr_topo.predict_proba(X_test[:, [0, 5]])[:, 1]
    prob_full = lr_cv.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]

    if len(set(test_y)) > 1:
        auc_results["Logistic_Topo"].append(roc_auc_score(test_y, prob_topo))
        auc_results["Logistic_Full"].append(roc_auc_score(test_y, prob_full))

# --- Summary table ---
auc_df = pd.DataFrame({
    "Model": list(auc_results.keys()),
    "Mean_AUC": [round(np.mean(v), 4) for v in auc_results.values()],
    "Std_AUC": [round(np.std(v), 4) for v in auc_results.values()]
})
auc_df.to_csv("outputs/model_comparison_auc.csv", index=False)
print("=== 5-Fold Cross-Validation AUC ===")
auc_df
```

### 2.5.5 Post-Disruption Rewiring Simulation

The resilience analysis in Section 2.6 shows what happens structurally when CJNG is removed: 24 nodes lost, 28 edges severed, the LCC cut in half. But that analysis treats the network as static. In practice, surviving organizations do not sit in the resulting fragments — they seek new alliances to replace lost connectivity, access new supply chains, or consolidate territory.

We use the logistic model fitted on the intact network to predict which new alliances are most likely to form after CJNG's removal. The procedure:

1. Remove CJNG and prune new isolates (reusing the post-removal graph from Section 2.6).
2. For every non-adjacent pair in the surviving network, recompute the 6 dyadic features on the post-removal topology. Node attributes (specialty, state) do not change; topological features (shared allies, degree product, balance) change because edges through CJNG no longer exist.
3. Score each pair with the fitted logistic model and rank by predicted probability.
4. The top-ranked pairs are the most plausible new alliances.

This is a strong assumption — that the same formation mechanisms that produced the original network also govern post-disruption reorganization. It is reasonable as a first approximation: geographic proximity, specialty complementarity, and shared enemies do not disappear when a hub falls. But it does not account for the possibility that disruption changes the rules of the game (e.g., new rivalries emerging among former CJNG allies competing for territory).

## 2.6 Topic 4: Network Resilience — Targeted Node Removal

A fundamental question in network science is: how does a network respond when a critical node is removed? This is known as network robustness analysis or attack tolerance [@albert2000error]. In the cartel context, node removal simulates real-world disruption events — the arrest, death, or incapacitation of a cartel leader and the consequent dissolution of that organization's alliances.

### Algorithmic Framework

The simulation is based on two classical network attack strategies from the robustness literature:

#### Algorithm 1: Single Targeted Node Removal

Given a graph $G = (V, E)$ and a target node $v^* \in V$:

1. **Snapshot pre-removal metrics** on $G$: compute $N$, $E$, density $\rho = \frac{2E}{N(N-1)}$, global clustering coefficient $C$, number of connected components $k$, size of the largest connected component (LCC), diameter $d$ and average shortest path length $\bar{\ell}$ of the LCC.
2. **Remove** $v^*$ and all its incident edges: $G' = G - \{v^*\}$, which deletes all edges $\{(v^*, u) : u \in \mathcal{N}(v^*)\}$ where $\mathcal{N}(v^*)$ is the neighborhood of $v^*$.
3. **Prune new isolates**: any node $u$ whose degree becomes 0 after removal (i.e., $u$ was connected to the rest of the network *only* through $v^*$) is also removed, since isolated nodes are not meaningful for structural analysis.
4. **Recompute all metrics** on $G'$ and report the difference $\Delta$.

The choice of **which** node to remove is guided by centrality. We target the node with the highest degree centrality $C_D(v) = \frac{\deg(v)}{N-1}$ — in this case CJNG (El Mencho), which is also the highest-betweenness node.

#### Algorithm 2: Sequential Targeted Attack (Iterative Hub Removal)

This extends Algorithm 1 to a sequence of $k$ removals and is the more realistic model of sustained law enforcement operations:

1. Set $G_0 = G$, $t = 0$.
2. **While** $t < k$ and $G_t$ is non-empty:
   - Compute degree of all nodes in $G_t$.
   - Select $v_t^* = \arg\max_{v \in V_t} \deg_t(v)$ (the current highest-degree node).
   - Remove $v_t^*$: $G_{t+1} = G_t - \{v_t^*\}$, prune new isolates.
   - Record $|LCC_{t+1}|$, number of components, and edges remaining.
   - $t \leftarrow t + 1$.
3. Plot the **fragmentation curve**: $|LCC_t|$ as a function of $t$.

Crucially, the degree is recalculated after each removal. This is an adaptive attack — the next target depends on the current graph state, not the original ranking. This is more realistic than removing all top-$k$ nodes simultaneously, because law enforcement operations unfold sequentially and the network reorganizes between each disruption.

### Why Scale-Free-Like Networks Are Especially Vulnerable

@albert2000error proved a fundamental asymmetry:

- **Random failure**: removing nodes uniformly at random has minimal impact on network connectivity, because most removed nodes are low-degree peripheral nodes.
- **Targeted attack**: removing the highest-degree node first causes maximum disruption per removal, because the hub's many edges are all severed simultaneously.

The mathematical intuition comes from the **percolation threshold**. In a network with degree distribution $P(k)$, the critical fraction of nodes that must be removed to destroy the giant component is:

$$f_c = 1 - \frac{1}{\kappa - 1}, \quad \text{where} \quad \kappa = \frac{\langle k^2 \rangle}{\langle k \rangle}$$

For heavy-tailed degree distributions, $\kappa$ is large (driven by high-degree hubs), so $f_c \to 1$ under random failure (very robust). But under targeted attack, each removal reduces $\kappa$ substantially, leading to rapid collapse.

### Metrics Tracked and Their Interpretation

| **Metric** | **Definition** | **What a change reveals** |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Nodes / Edges** | $N$, $E$ after removal | Direct loss of actors and connections |
| **Density** | $\rho = 2E / (N(N-1))$ | Whether remaining nodes are more or less interconnected |
| **Clustering $C$** | $C = \frac{3 \times \text{triangles}}{\text{connected triples}}$ | Whether local cohesion (triadic closure) increases or decreases |
| **Components** | Number of connected components $k$ | Network fragmentation — higher $k$ means more isolated subgroups |
| **LCC Size** | Nodes in the largest connected component | How much of the network remains reachable after disruption |
| **Diameter** | $d = \max_{u,v} d(u,v)$ in LCC | Maximum communication distance in the surviving network |
| **Avg Path Length** | $\bar{\ell} = \frac{1}{n(n-1)} \sum_{u \neq v} d(u,v)$ in LCC | Average reachability — higher $\bar{\ell}$ means slower coordination |

### Assumptions

1. **Node ≈ Organization**: We equate removing a node with neutralizing the entire cartel organization it represents. In reality, killing a leader (El Mencho) does not instantly dissolve CJNG — but it does sever the decision-making center that maintained alliances, making this a reasonable first-order approximation.
2. **Alliance-only graph**: We perform the simulation on the positive-ties subgraph only. Rivalry edges are excluded because we are interested in how the *cooperation structure* fragments. In practice, rivalry ties might actually intensify after a hub removal (rival groups may attack the weakened remnants).
3. **Static removal**: We assume no rewiring — once CJNG is removed, its former allies do not immediately form new alliances with each other. This is a conservative (worst-case) assumption; in reality, some re-alignment would occur over time.
4. **Isolate pruning**: Nodes that become completely disconnected after the removal are pruned from analysis. These represent cartels that had *no other alliances* besides the removed node — they effectively exit the cooperation network.
5. **Adaptive sequential attack**: In the sequential removal, we re-rank by degree after each step rather than using the original ranking. This models a realistic strategy where law enforcement adapts to the evolving network.
6. **Undirected, unweighted edges for topology**: While the original data has alliance weights (indicating alliance strength across states), the topological algorithms (degree, betweenness, clustering, path length) treat all edges as unweighted. This focuses the analysis on structural position rather than tie strength.

## 2.7 Optimal Targeting Under Rewiring

### The Problem

The resilience analysis in Section 2.6 and the rewiring simulation in Section 2.5.5 treat node selection and post-disruption reorganization as separate steps. But if the goal is to choose which organizations to target, the two must be considered jointly: the optimal target set depends on how the network reorganizes afterward.

We formalize this as a combinatorial optimization problem. Let $S \subseteq V$ be a set of nodes removed simultaneously, with $|S| \leq k$ where $k$ is the operational budget — the number of organizations that law enforcement can neutralize in a single coordinated operation. After removal, surviving organizations form new alliances according to the logistic model from Section 2.5.3. The objective is to choose $S$ to minimize the largest connected component in the post-rewiring network:

$$S^* = \arg\min_{S \subseteq V,\; |S| = k} \; \text{LCC}\Big(\text{Rewire}\big(G \setminus S,\; \hat{P},\; \tau\big)\Big)$$

The function $\text{Rewire}(G', \hat{P}, \tau)$ takes the surviving graph $G' = G \setminus S$ (after pruning isolates), scores every non-adjacent pair with the logistic model, adds edges whose predicted probability exceeds the threshold $\tau$, and returns the reorganized graph.

### Threshold Calibration

The threshold $\tau$ controls how aggressively the network rewires. We calibrate it through a parameter $\alpha \in (0, 1)$ that represents the rewiring capacity — the fraction of lost edges that the surviving network can replace. For a given removal set $S$:

1. Count the edges lost: $E_{\text{lost}} = |E(G)| - |E(G \setminus S)|$
2. On the surviving graph, score all non-edges with the logistic model
3. Set $\tau$ so that the number of non-edges exceeding $\tau$ equals $\alpha \cdot E_{\text{lost}}$

We run the full analysis at $\alpha = 0.25, 0.50, 0.75$ to show sensitivity. Low $\alpha$ represents a hostile enforcement environment where new alliances are hard to form; high $\alpha$ represents a permissive one.

### Exhaustive Search

For $k = 1$, there are 91 candidate removals. Each evaluation involves scoring $\sim N^2/2$ non-edges with the logistic model, which takes milliseconds. We solve the problem exactly by exhaustive enumeration.

### Baselines

We compare the optimal target set against four baselines:

- Degree-based: remove the top-$k$ nodes by degree centrality
- Betweenness-based: remove the top-$k$ nodes by betweenness centrality
- Degree + rewiring: same targets as degree-based, but allow rewiring
- Random: average post-rewiring LCC over 200 random $k$-subsets

The comparison between "degree + rewiring" and "optimal" shows the cost of ignoring rewiring when choosing targets.

```{python}
from itertools import combinations

def rewire_and_measure(G_orig, removal_set, lr_model, G_riv, G_sign,
                       alpha, feature_func):
    """Remove nodes, rewire, return post-rewiring LCC size."""
    # Build subgraph without copies — use subgraph view then materialize only edges
    surviving = set(G_orig.nodes()) - removal_set
    G_rem = G_orig.subgraph(surviving).copy()

    # Prune isolates
    iso = [n for n in G_rem.nodes() if G_rem.degree(n) == 0]
    G_rem.remove_nodes_from(iso)

    if G_rem.number_of_nodes() == 0:
        return 0

    # Count lost edges
    e_lost = G_orig.number_of_edges() - G_rem.number_of_edges()
    n_new_edges = max(1, int(alpha * e_lost))

    # Score all non-edges — build feature matrix in bulk
    rem_nodes = sorted(G_rem.nodes())
    edge_set = set(G_rem.edges())
    non_edges = []
    for i, u in enumerate(rem_nodes):
        for v in rem_nodes[i+1:]:
            if (u, v) not in edge_set and (v, u) not in edge_set:
                non_edges.append((u, v))

    if len(non_edges) == 0:
        comps = list(nx.connected_components(G_rem))
        return max(len(c) for c in comps) if comps else 0

    # Precompute neighbor sets for speed
    all_neighbors = {n: set(G_rem.neighbors(n)) for n in rem_nodes}
    riv_neighbors = {n: set(G_riv.neighbors(n)) if G_riv.has_node(n) else set()
                     for n in rem_nodes}
    sign_neighbors = {n: set(G_sign.neighbors(n)) if G_sign.has_node(n) else set()
                      for n in rem_nodes}

    rows = []
    for u, v in non_edges:
        nu, nv = all_neighbors[u], all_neighbors[v]
        shared_allies = len(nu & nv)
        shared_rivals = len(riv_neighbors[u] & riv_neighbors[v])

        su = node_specialty.get(u, "Undocumented")
        sv = node_specialty.get(v, "Undocumented")
        comp = int(su != sv and su != "Undocumented" and sv != "Undocumented")
        same_st = int(node_state.get(u, "") == node_state.get(v, "")
                      and node_state.get(u, "") != "")

        balance = 0
        for w in sign_neighbors[u] & sign_neighbors[v]:
            wt_uw = G_sign[u][w].get("weight", 0)
            wt_vw = G_sign[v][w].get("weight", 0)
            balance += 1 if wt_uw * wt_vw > 0 else -1

        deg_prod = G_rem.degree(u) * G_rem.degree(v)
        rows.append([shared_allies, shared_rivals, comp, same_st, balance, deg_prod])

    X_re = np.array(rows)
    probs = lr_model.predict_proba(X_re)[:, 1]

    # Add top n_new_edges by probability
    top_idx = np.argsort(probs)[::-1][:n_new_edges]
    for idx in top_idx:
        u, v = non_edges[idx]
        G_rem.add_edge(u, v)

    comps = list(nx.connected_components(G_rem))
    return max(len(c) for c in comps) if comps else 0
```

The log-log degree distribution plot (@fig-degree-dist) compares the three networks. The ER graph produces a tight Poisson-like cluster where most nodes have similar degree — no hubs, no long tail. This degree heterogeneity is what makes the network vulnerable to targeted attack, as we show in Section 3.8.

In other words, these pairs already operate in the same alliance orbit; the only thing missing is the direct edge between them.

The Adamic-Adar index serves as a tiebreaker among these perfect-Jaccard pairs. Ántrax and Mando Sur Gente Nueva score highest (AA = 1.44) because their shared neighbor has degree 2 — a selective, exclusive connection that carries more information than a shared neighbor with degree 20. Pairs sharing a higher-degree intermediary (like those connected through CJNG or a regional cartel) score lower on Adamic-Adar because that intermediary allies with everyone and therefore provides weaker evidence of a special relationship.

Several of these predicted pairs make substantive sense. Ántrax and Mando Sur Gente Nueva are both armed wings within the Sinaloa ecosystem — their lack of a direct alliance edge may reflect an intelligence gap rather than a genuine absence. The Caballeros Templarios cluster (pairs 5–10) involves a regional cartel and several cells in Michoacán that share a common ally but lack pairwise ties. These are the kind of alliances that geographic proximity and operational overlap would tend to produce over time.

@berlusconi2016link found that equivalent topological predictions in Italian Mafia networks were later confirmed by intelligence agencies. In our context, these predictions identify either hidden alliances that exist but were not captured in the 2020 snapshot, or alliances that are likely to form as the organizations continue to operate in the same territory with the same intermediaries.

The score distributions (@fig-link-pred-dist) show that the vast majority of non-edge pairs score zero on both metrics — they share no neighbors at all. The top-15 pairs are clear outliers sitting at the far right tail of both distributions.

Removing CJNG drops the network from 91 to 67 nodes — a loss of 24 organizations, not just the one removed. CJNG itself accounts for 1 node, but its removal turns 23 of its former allies into isolates (nodes with no remaining alliances). These 23 organizations were connected to the rest of the network only through CJNG. They had no other alliances. When the hub disappears, they disappear from the cooperation network entirely.

Edges drop from 91 to 63 — a loss of 28 alliance ties, or 31% of all edges in the network. The 28 lost edges include CJNG's own alliances plus the edges among the newly isolated nodes.

The LCC shrinks from 50 to 26. Before removal, the main cluster held a slim majority of the network. After removal, it holds less than a third. The number of connected components rises from 8 to 10, but this understates the fragmentation — the 23 new isolates are pruned entirely, so the effective splintering is much worse than a +2 component count suggests.

Two metrics move in a counterintuitive direction. Density increases from 0.022 to 0.029, and clustering rises from 0.036 to 0.063. This does not mean the network got stronger. It means the loosely connected periphery — the nodes hanging on through CJNG — was pruned away, leaving behind a smaller, denser core of organizations that are mutually allied. The surviving network is more tightly knit precisely because the fragile fringe was amputated.

The diameter drops from 8 to 3 and average path length from 3.30 to 2.14. Again, this is not a sign of improved connectivity — it reflects the fact that the surviving LCC is a compact cluster where everyone is close to everyone else, because the nodes that were far away (reachable only through CJNG) are no longer part of it.

The side-by-side visualization (@fig-node-removal) shows this plainly. The left panel has CJNG (red) at the center with spokes radiating outward to its allies (orange). The right panel shows those allies either gone or pushed to the periphery, with a smaller, denser residual network.

## 4.1 Answers to Research Questions

RQ1: The cartel alliance network has 91 non-isolate organizations connected by 91 alliance edges across 8 components. The structure is sparse ($\rho = 0.022$) and heavily concentrated around two national cartels — CJNG and Sinaloa — which together account for a disproportionate share of all edges. Organizational type tracks network position: national cartels hold hub and broker roles, while local groups and cells sit at the periphery, often connected through a single tie to a national hub.

RQ2: The alliance network does not match either null model. It clusters more than Erdős–Rényi (which produces zero triangles at this density) and is more fragmented than Barabási–Albert (which produces a single connected component). Both null models also produce longer average path lengths. The network's combination of high clustering, short paths, and multi-component fragmentation points to mechanisms — geographic proximity, triadic closure through trust, shared rivalry pressure — that random wiring cannot replicate.

RQ3: Topological link prediction (Jaccard, Adamic-Adar) identifies 15 pairs with identical alliance neighborhoods that lack a direct tie. The dyadic utility logistic regression extends this by incorporating rivalry structure, specialty complementarity, geographic proximity, and structural balance alongside topology. Cross-validation shows whether these non-topological features improve prediction over topology alone. The logistic coefficients reveal what drives alliance formation: shared allies (triadic closure), shared enemies (balance theory), and complementary specialties (supply-chain logic) each carry interpretable weight.

RQ4: Removing CJNG eliminates 28 edges (31% of the total), turns 23 organizations into isolates, and cuts the LCC from 50 to 26. But the network does not stay fragmented. The post-disruption rewiring simulation predicts which new alliances form in the surviving network, addressing whether hub removal dismantles the network or merely triggers reorganization.

RQ5: The optimal targeting analysis solves the problem exactly for $k = 1$ across three rewiring capacity levels. The results show whether the naive strategy (target the highest-degree nodes) remains optimal once rewiring is accounted for, or whether less obvious targets — brokers, geographic bridges — produce more durable fragmentation by leaving surviving organizations without the conditions (shared state, shared enemies) needed to form new alliances.

## 4.2 Substantive Interpretation

Two organizations — CJNG and Sinaloa — hold the alliance network together. Between them they are allied with roughly half the non-isolate nodes. This concentration of connectivity around two rival hubs creates a bipolar structure: most smaller organizations are satellites of one hub or the other, and the rivalry between the two hubs (weight = 7, the maximum in the dataset) defines the principal axis of conflict.

The brokerage analysis complicates this picture. Several local organizations (Talibanes, Unión Tepito, El Abuelo, Viagras) rank high on betweenness despite having few alliances. They occupy bottleneck positions connecting subgraphs that have no alternative path between them. Disrupting these brokers would fragment communication channels in ways that raw degree counts do not predict — a point made by @duijn2014relative in the context of Dutch criminal networks.

The specialty data shows that the alliance network connects organizations with different criminal portfolios. National drug trafficking cartels ally with local extortion groups, toll-collectors, and cells. This looks more like functional complementarity (allying with organizations that offer different capabilities) than homophily (allying with similar organizations). The geographic analysis reinforces this: cross-state alliances link production regions to distribution corridors, suggesting that the alliance network partly maps supply-chain relationships.

The temporal crime trends frame the stakes. The BACRIM 2020 snapshot was taken during a period of nearly 37,000 annual homicides, up sharply from 2017. That escalation coincides with the fragmentation of major cartels under the kingpin strategy — the same kind of hub removal our simulation models. @calderon2015beheading documented the mechanism: decapitation creates power vacuums that produce violence as factions compete for control. Our node-removal results quantify the structural damage, but they do not capture the violence that fills the vacuum. Fragmentation is not the same as pacification.

## 4.3 Limitations

The main limitation is temporal. The 2020 snapshot captures alliances and rivalries at a single point in time. We cannot observe how ties form, dissolve, or shift in response to arrests, killings, or territorial changes. A longitudinal dataset would allow testing whether link predictions materialize and whether the fragmentation dynamics we simulate actually play out as modeled.

The logistic link prediction model incorporates node attributes (specialty, geography) and signed network features (shared rivals, balance), but it still uses a static cross-section. Temporal features — alliance duration, recent enforcement actions, evolving territorial control — are unavailable in the 2020 snapshot and would likely improve prediction if longitudinal data existed.

The data is necessarily incomplete. Covert relationships may go unrecorded, and the boundary of who counts as a distinct organization is sometimes blurry. @sparrow1991application identified incompleteness and fuzzy boundaries as fundamental challenges of criminal network analysis, and they apply here.

We treat each organization as a unitary actor, but internal factions (Los Chapitos vs. Los Mayos within Sinaloa, for instance) may have distinct and even conflicting alliance structures. The 2024 Sinaloa civil war demonstrates this limitation in practice.

Finally, our algorithms treat all alliance edges as equal. The original data includes alliance weights (number of states where the alliance is active), which could proxy for tie strength. A weighted analysis might produce different centrality rankings and different link predictions.

## 4.4 Future Work

Temporal network analysis over multiple BACRIM snapshots would allow tracking of alliance evolution and direct testing of both the topological and logistic link predictions. The logistic model could be extended with temporal covariates (time since last enforcement action, alliance age) if longitudinal data became available. Signed-link prediction — predicting both future alliances and future rivalries from the same framework — is a natural extension. ERGMs or STERGMs could test the same hypotheses within a formal statistical network framework that accounts for dyadic dependencies. The optimal targeting framework could be extended to sequential (multi-round) operations where the network rewires between each targeting step, and to multi-objective formulations that trade off fragmentation against predicted violence intensity. Agent-based simulation of post-disruption rewiring, where organizations make sequential alliance decisions rather than simultaneous ones, would add dynamic realism to the static rewiring prediction.
