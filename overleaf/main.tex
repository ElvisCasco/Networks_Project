% --- Abstract ---
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper analyzes the BACRIM 2020 signed network of 150 Mexican criminal organizations using centrality analysis, null-model comparison, link prediction, and targeted node-removal simulation. The alliance network is sparse, clustered above random baselines, and dominated by two hubs (CJNG and Sinaloa). A dyadic logistic model incorporating geography, shared rivalries, and specialty outperforms topology-only link prediction (AUC 0.73 vs.\ chance). Targeted removal of CJNG fragments the network into multiple disconnected components, consistent with theoretical predictions for heavy-tailed topologies. Events between 2024 and 2026, including the arrest of El Mayo Zambada, the killing of El Mencho, and mass extraditions, track the structural vulnerabilities flagged by this static 2020 snapshot.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Context and Motivation}

Organized crime is a relational phenomenon. Alliances and rivalries among criminal organizations shape who controls territory, who moves product, and where violence concentrates. In Mexico, drug cartels form webs of cooperation and competition that have contributed to over 290,000 homicides and 175,000 disappearances between 2012 and 2021 \citep{inegi2022}. Understanding the structure of these webs is presented here as an academic exercise grounded in real policing challenges, with potential relevance for disruption strategy, intelligence prioritization, and policy design.

The BACRIM 2020 dataset \citep{prietocuriel2023bacrim} provides a strong empirical base, a signed network of 150 Mexican criminal organizations where positive ties denote alliances and negative ties denote rivalries. Yet a static map alone cannot answer key analytical questions. Is the observed structure systematic or random? Which currently disconnected pairs are most likely to form future alliances? How does the network change when high-centrality actors are removed?

Prior work provides the methodological foundation for addressing these questions. Network studies of criminal organizations emphasize brokerage, clustering, and degree heterogeneity as core structural features \citep{morselli2009inside, sparrow1991application}. Signed-network research formalizes balance mechanisms relevant to alliance-rivalry systems \citep{heider1946attitudes, cartwright1956structural, leskovec2010predicting}. Robustness literature explains why hub-targeted interventions can produce disproportionate fragmentation \citep{albert2000error, duijn2014relative}. Link-prediction research defines the topological metrics used to infer likely future ties \citep{libennowell2007link, berlusconi2016link}. For Mexico specifically, BACRIM and related studies provide empirical context on territorial competition and fragmentation dynamics \citep{prietocuriel2023bacrim, coscia2012knowing, calderon2015beheading}.

Our contribution is to analyze a contemporary cartel network dataset, enriched with specialty attributes from DEA assessments and open-source intelligence, using graph-topological analysis, null-model benchmarking, alliance link prediction, and targeted-disruption simulations to address the following research problems:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RP\arabic*:}, leftmargin=2.5em]
    \item What structural properties characterize the cartel alliance network, and how does it differ from random baselines?
    \item Which cartel pairs are most likely to form future alliances, and do non-topological features (geography, rivalry, specialty) improve prediction over topology alone?
    \item How does targeted removal of central hubs fragment the network, and how does the surviving network plausibly reorganize?
\end{enumerate}

This paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data_main} describes the data and contextual overview. Section~\ref{sec:methods} presents the analytical methods. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports findings on network structure, alliance prediction, and disruption scenarios. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets these results in light of recent developments, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with limitations and future directions.

\section{Data and Context}\label{sec:data_main}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Collection and Preprocessing}\label{sec:data}

We utilize the BACRIM 2020 dataset \citep{prietocuriel2023bacrim}, a publicly available criminal-network dataset that provides node and edge information for Mexican cartel organizations. The dataset includes three primary files:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Nodes.csv} --- (node data) 150 cartel organizations with group name, state, and short name.
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Alliances.csv} --- (edge data) alliance ties with positive weights representing the number of Mexican states where the alliance is active.
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Rivals.csv} --- (edge data) rivalry ties with negative weights.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Because BACRIM 2020 provides the core network structure but not all attributes required for our analyses (e.g., organizational specialties and broader violence context), we supplement it with the following additional sources:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Nodes\_With\_Specialties.csv} --- (node data) node-level content that extends BACRIM organizations with organizational type (National Cartel, Regional Cartel, Local, Cell, Toll-Collector), primary specialty, activity list, and drug portfolio. These specialty attributes were compiled from multiple external references and harmonized to BACRIM entities, with row-level source attribution (e.g., DEA NDTA 2024/2025, CRS report R41576, and InSight Crime profiles \citep{dea2024,dea2025,crsR41576,insightcrimeProfiles}).
    \item \texttt{Trends2012\_2021.csv} --- (context data, non-network) annual counts of homicides, missing persons, and arrests in Mexico from INEGI \citep{inegi2022}.
\end{itemize}

\noindent To build the dataset, we first combine the two BACRIM 2020 edge datasets (alliances and rivalries). Each record is mapped to a common organization list, producing a unified tie table with a consistent node reference scheme. Next, we remove duplicate edges by writing each undirected pair in a fixed order $(\min(u,v), \max(u,v))$, so repeated or reversed pairs are treated as the same relationship. Isolate nodes (organizations with no alliance ties) are removed from the alliance-only network for structural analysis. Finally, we construct two graphs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{$G_{\text{signed}}$} (Signed Master Graph): an undirected graph where each alliance edge carries weight $+1$ and each rivalry edge carries weight $-1$.
    \item \textbf{$G_{\text{alliance}}$} (Alliance Subgraph): the positive-edge subgraph used for centrality, link prediction, and resilience tasks.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent All code, data, and outputs required to replicate this study are available at \url{https://github.com/ElvisCasco/cartel-alliance-prediction}.

Three patterns in this overview shape the methodology that follows. \subsection{Stage 1: Structural Characterization}\label{sec:stage1_methods}

Addressing RP1 requires identifying which organizations occupy structurally critical positions and determining whether the observed topology departs from random baselines. We apply centrality analysis for the former and null-model comparison for the latter.

\subsubsection{Centrality Measures}\label{sec:centrality_methods}

We compute two centrality measures on $G_{\text{alliance}}$. Degree centrality identifies hubs (many alliances), while betweenness centrality identifies brokers (nodes that bridge otherwise disconnected clusters, even if their own alliance count is modest).

For a node $v$ in an undirected graph with $N$ nodes, degree centrality normalizes the number of incident edges by the maximum possible:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:degree}
    C_D(v) = \frac{k_v}{N - 1}
\end{equation}
where $k_v = \sum_{j} A_{vj}$. Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on shortest paths between other pairs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betweenness}
    C_B(v) = \sum_{\substack{s \neq v \neq t \\ s, t \in V}} \frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{st}$ is the total number of shortest paths from $s$ to $t$ and $\sigma_{st}(v)$ is the number passing through $v$. We normalize by $\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}$ and use Brandes' algorithm \citep{brandes2001faster}.

\subsubsection{Null Models}\label{sec:null_models}

Sparsity or clustering is only informative if it differs from what random wiring would produce. We benchmark $G_{\text{alliance}}$ against two null models matched on $N$ and expected $E$, one based on purely random attachment (Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi) and one based on preferential attachment to high-degree nodes (Barab\'asi--Albert).

In the Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi $G(N,p)$ model, each of the $\binom{N}{2}$ possible edges exists independently with probability $p = 2E_{\text{real}} / (N(N-1))$, producing a Poisson degree distribution and expected clustering $C_{\text{ER}} = p$ \citep{erdos1959random}. In the Barab\'asi--Albert model, each new node attaches to $m$ existing nodes with probability proportional to current degree, producing a power-law distribution $P(k) \sim k^{-3}$; we set $m = \text{round}(E/N)$ to match the average degree \citep{barabasi1999emergence}. Following \citet{watts1998collective}, we compute the small-world coefficient:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smallworld}
    \sigma = \frac{C_{\text{real}} / C_{\text{ER}}}{L_{\text{real}} / L_{\text{ER}}}
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the global clustering coefficient and $L$ is the average shortest path length in the largest connected component. A value $\sigma > 1$ indicates small-world properties: high local clustering combined with short global paths.

\subsection{Stage 2: Link Prediction}\label{sec:stage2_methods}

Given that the alliance structure is not random, the forces that shaped existing ties should help predict future ones. The outcome we predict is alliance formation, whether a currently unconnected pair will form a cooperative tie. Rivalry information enters not as an outcome but as a predictor, with shared enemies and signed-balance scores from $G_{\text{signed}}$ serving as features in the logistic model. We first apply two topological similarity metrics on $G_{\text{alliance}}$ alone, then fit a dyadic logistic model that adds rivalry-derived features alongside geography and specialty. If the topological metrics already perform well, non-network attributes add little value. If they do not, contextual factors are necessary and a richer model is justified.

\subsubsection{Topological Metrics}\label{sec:topo_metrics}

We predict future alliances among currently non-adjacent pairs using two topological similarity metrics \citep{libennowell2007link}. The Jaccard coefficient measures the fraction of the combined neighborhood that is shared:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jaccard}
    J(u, v) = \frac{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)|}{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cup \mathcal{N}(v)|}
\end{equation}
The Adamic-Adar index weights each shared neighbor inversely by the log of its degree, so that low-degree shared neighbors contribute more \citep{adamic2003friends}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adamicadar}
    AA(u, v) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)} \frac{1}{\log |\mathcal{N}(w)|}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Dyadic Logistic Model}\label{sec:logistic_methods}

Topology-only metrics ignore geography, shared enemies, and operational complementarity. To test whether these factors improve prediction, we treat each potential pair $(i,j)$ as a binary outcome and fit a logistic regression:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:logistic}
    P(\text{alliance} \mid i, j) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta_{ij}}}
\end{equation}
We define six dyadic features, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:features} and detailed below.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Dyadic features for alliance prediction.}
\label{tab:features}
\small
\begin{tabular}{cllll}
\toprule
\textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Source} & \textbf{Type} & \textbf{Mechanism} \\
\midrule
$x_1$ & SharedAllies & $G_{\text{alliance}}$ & Count & Triadic closure \\
$x_2$ & SharedRivals & $G_{\text{signed}}$ (rivalry edges) & Count & Structural balance \\
$x_3$ & Complementarity & Node attributes & Binary & Specialty heterophily \\
$x_4$ & SameState & Node attributes & Binary & Geographic proximity \\
$x_5$ & BalanceScore & $G_{\text{signed}}$ (all edges) & Integer & Heider balance \\
$x_6$ & DegreeProduct & $G_{\text{alliance}}$ & Count & Preferential attachment \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent $x_1$ (SharedAllies). $|\mathcal{N}_{\text{alliance}}(i) \cap \mathcal{N}_{\text{alliance}}(j)|$, the number of organizations allied with both $i$ and $j$. Triadic closure predicts that pairs sharing many allies face social pressure toward direct cooperation.

$x_2$ (SharedRivals). $|\mathcal{N}_{\text{rivalry}}(i) \cap \mathcal{N}_{\text{rivalry}}(j)|$, the number of organizations that are rivals of both $i$ and $j$, computed on the rivalry-only subgraph extracted from $G_{\text{signed}}$. Structural balance theory predicts that shared enemies push organizations toward alliance (``the enemy of my enemy is my friend'').

$x_3$ (Complementarity). A binary indicator equal to 1 if $i$ and $j$ have different documented primary specialties, 0 otherwise. Pairs where either organization has an undocumented specialty are coded 0. The primary specialties in the dataset are Drug Trafficking, Drug Retail, Extortion, and Fuel Theft. This feature tests whether organizations with different criminal profiles are more likely to ally, for example a trafficking organization pairing with an extortion group to secure territory.

$x_4$ (SameState). A binary indicator equal to 1 if both organizations operate in the same Mexican state. Geographic proximity lowers coordination costs and creates shared territorial incentives.

$x_5$ (BalanceScore). For each common neighbor $w$ in $G_{\text{signed}}$, we add $+1$ if both edges $(i,w)$ and $(j,w)$ carry the same sign (both alliances or both rivalries), and $-1$ if they carry opposite signs. The sum captures Heider balance. Triads where all three pairs are consistent ($+/+/+$ or $-/-/+$) contribute positively, while imbalanced triads contribute negatively.

$x_6$ (DegreeProduct). $\deg_{\text{alliance}}(i) \times \deg_{\text{alliance}}(j)$, the product of alliance-graph degrees. This operationalizes preferential attachment, where highly connected organizations are more likely to form new ties with each other.

\noindent We fit two nested specifications to isolate the contribution of non-topological features:
\begin{align}
    \text{Topology-only:} \quad \eta_{ij}^{\text{topo}} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_6 x_6 \label{eq:logistic_topo} \\[4pt]
    \text{Full model:} \quad \eta_{ij}^{\text{full}} &= \beta_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{6} \beta_k x_k \label{eq:logistic_full}
\end{align}
The topology-only model uses SharedAllies and DegreeProduct, features derived entirely from $G_{\text{alliance}}$. The full model adds rivalry, geographic, and specialty features from $G_{\text{signed}}$ and node attributes. With 91 edges among $\binom{91}{2} = 4{,}095$ possible pairs (2.2\% positive rate), we use class-weighted logistic regression to prevent trivial all-zero predictions.

\subsubsection{Evaluation}\label{sec:eval_methods}

To compare the four models (Jaccard, Adamic-Adar, topology-only logistic, full logistic), we use 5-fold edge-holdout cross-validation. The 91 alliance edges are randomly partitioned into five folds. In each iteration, one fold (20\% of edges) is held out and removed from $G_{\text{alliance}}$, and all four models are trained on the remaining graph. Features are recomputed on this reduced graph so that no model has access to the held-out edges during training. For evaluation, the held-out edges serve as true positives and are paired with an equal number of randomly sampled non-edges as true negatives. Each model scores the test pairs, with Jaccard and Adamic-Adar producing raw similarity values and the logistic models producing predicted probabilities. We measure performance using AUC-ROC, where 0.5 corresponds to random guessing and 1.0 to perfect separation of real alliances from non-edges, and report the mean and standard deviation across folds.

\subsection{Stage 3: Network Resilience}\label{sec:stage3_methods}

The centrality analysis shows that connectivity depends on a handful of hubs. We now ask what happens when those hubs are removed. We simulate targeted node removal on $G_{\text{alliance}}$, first a single hub, then the top hubs sequentially, to measure how quickly the cooperation structure fragments (RP3). Rivalry edges are excluded because we are interested in how the alliance network breaks apart, not in the rivalry topology.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:single} describes single targeted removal and Algorithm~\ref{alg:sequential} describes sequential adaptive attack, where degree is recalculated after each removal to reflect the evolving topology. This adaptive approach is more realistic than simultaneous removal since law enforcement operations unfold sequentially.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Single targeted removal}\label{alg:single}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $G_{\text{alliance}}$
\State $v^* \gets \arg\max_{v} C_D(v)$ \Comment{highest-degree node}
\State Record pre-removal metrics ($N, E, \rho, C, $ components, LCC, diameter, $L$)
\State Remove $v^*$ and all incident edges from $G_{\text{alliance}}$
\State Prune new isolates
\State Record post-removal metrics
\State \Return $\Delta$ between pre- and post-removal metrics
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Sequential adaptive attack}\label{alg:sequential}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $G_{\text{alliance}}$, budget $k$
\State $G_0 \gets G_{\text{alliance}}$
\For{$t = 1, \ldots, k$}
    \State $v_t \gets \arg\max_{v \in G_{t-1}} \deg_{G_{t-1}}(v)$ \Comment{recalculate degree}
    \State Remove $v_t$ and all incident edges from $G_{t-1}$
    \State Prune new isolates to obtain $G_t$
    \State Record LCC size and component count of $G_t$
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\citet{albert2000error} showed that scale-free-like networks are robust to random failure but fragile under targeted attack. The critical removal fraction is $f_c = 1 - 1/(\kappa - 1)$, where $\kappa = \langle k^2 \rangle / \langle k \rangle$; for heavy-tailed distributions, $\kappa$ is large and targeted removal rapidly causes collapse. We equate node removal with organizational neutralization, use the alliance-only graph, assume no rewiring after removal, prune isolates, and treat edges as unweighted.

\subsection{Stage 4: Optimal Targeting Under Rewiring}\label{sec:stage4_methods}

Stage~3 holds the network static after removal, but surviving organizations seek new alliances to replace lost connections. A target that fragments the static network may be suboptimal if survivors rewire around the gap. We therefore combine the removal framework with the logistic model from Stage~2: for each candidate target set, we remove the nodes from $G_{\text{alliance}}$, predict new alliances using the model (which draws on rivalry features from $G_{\text{signed}}$), and evaluate the post-rewiring fragmentation.

We formalize this as a combinatorial optimization problem. Let $S \subseteq V$ with $|S| = k$ be the set of nodes removed simultaneously, where $k$ is the operational budget. After removal, surviving organizations form new alliances according to the logistic model. The objective is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal}
    S^* = \arg\min_{S \subseteq V,\; |S| = k} \; \text{LCC}\Big(\text{Rewire}\big(G \setminus S,\; \hat{P},\; \tau\big)\Big)
\end{equation}
The parameter $\alpha \in \{0.25, 0.50, 0.75\}$ represents rewiring capacity, the fraction of lost edges the network can replace. Low $\alpha$ models a hostile enforcement environment and high $\alpha$ models a permissive one. Algorithm~\ref{alg:optimal} describes the procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Optimal targeting under rewiring}\label{alg:optimal}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $G_{\text{alliance}}$, $G_{\text{signed}}$, logistic model $\hat{P}$, budget $k$, rewiring capacity $\alpha$
\For{each candidate set $S \subseteq V$ with $|S| = k$}
    \State $G' \gets G_{\text{alliance}} \setminus S$ \Comment{remove target nodes and incident edges}
    \State Prune isolates from $G'$
    \State $E_{\text{lost}} \gets E_{\text{original}} - E_{G'}$
    \State Score all non-adjacent pairs in $G'$ using $\hat{P}$ with features from $G'$ and $G_{\text{signed}}$
    \State Add the top $\lfloor \alpha \cdot E_{\text{lost}} \rfloor$ predicted edges to $G'$
    \State $\text{LCC}(S) \gets$ size of largest connected component of $G'$
\EndFor
\State \Return $S^* = \arg\min_{S} \text{LCC}(S)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

For $k = 1$, there are 91 candidate removals and each evaluation involves scoring $\sim N^2/2$ non-edges, so we solve the problem exactly by exhaustive enumeration. We compare the optimal set against four baselines: top-$k$ by degree, top-$k$ by betweenness, degree-based with rewiring, and the mean over 200 random $k$-subsets.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}\label{sec:results_summary}

The four stages yield a consistent picture. The alliance network is sparse, hub-dominated, and clustered above random baselines (Stage~1). Topology alone cannot predict alliance formation, as geography and shared enemies are the primary drivers, with the full logistic model achieving AUC 0.73 against chance-level performance for all other models (Stage~2). Removing CJNG eliminates 31\% of edges and halves the largest component (Stage~3), and the sequential fragmentation curve matches theoretical predictions for heavy-tailed networks. When the surviving network is allowed to reorganize, the logistic model predicts rewiring patterns that may partially restore connectivity, raising the question of whether static targeting strategies remain optimal (Stage~4).

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

The results paint a bipolar cooperation structure held together by two national hubs. CJNG and Sinaloa account for over half the alliance ties among non-isolate nodes, and their rivalry (weight 7, the dataset maximum) defines the principal axis of conflict. Most smaller organizations are satellites of one hub or the other. Yet degree is not the only dimension that matters: local organizations such as Talibanes and Uni\'on Tepito rank high on betweenness despite few alliances, occupying bottleneck positions whose disruption would fragment communication channels in ways that degree alone does not predict \citep{duijn2014relative}. Cross-state alliances link production regions to distribution corridors, partly mapping supply-chain relationships, while same-state alliances reflect territorial cooperation. The logistic model confirms this pattern, with SameState as the dominant predictor of alliance formation (OR $= 5.66$, $p < 0.001$).

The link prediction results carry a methodological implication. In sparse, hub-dominated networks, topological similarity metrics perform at chance because most pairs share zero neighbors. Contextual features such as geography and shared enemies carry the predictive signal. This is consistent with \citet{berlusconi2016link}, who found that attribute-enriched models outperformed topology-only baselines in Italian Mafia networks. The practical consequence is that intelligence analysts predicting future alliances should prioritize geographic proximity and rivalry structure over alliance topology.

The node-removal simulations quantify what the centrality analysis implies. Removing CJNG halves the largest component and isolates 23 satellite organizations; sequential removal of the top three hubs captures most of the achievable fragmentation. This matches the theoretical prediction for heavy-tailed networks \citep{albert2000error} and aligns with the real-world enforcement strategy observed between 2024 and 2026. However, \citet{calderon2015beheading} documented that decapitation creates power vacuums that produce violence. Our simulations quantify structural damage but do not capture the violence that fills the vacuum. Fragmentation is not pacification.

Events since 2020 track the structural vulnerabilities identified in our analysis. Ismael ``El Mayo'' Zambada, co-founder of Sinaloa (the second-highest degree node), was arrested in Texas on July 25, 2024, triggering a civil war between Los Chapitos and Zambada loyalists. In network terms, one high-degree node split into competing lower-degree nodes with alliance edges replaced by rivalry edges. On February 22, 2026, Mexican military forces killed Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes (``El Mencho''), CJNG's leader and the highest-degree, highest-betweenness node in the 2020 network. Our simulation predicts that removing CJNG drops the LCC from 50 to 26 and turns 23 satellites into isolates. The immediate retaliatory attacks in Jalisco are consistent with hub removal in a centralized network. In February 2025, the US designated both CJNG and Sinaloa as Foreign Terrorist Organizations, and mass extraditions (29 figures in February, 26 more in August 2025) dismantled Los Cuinis, CJNG's strongest ally, before El Mencho's elimination. The factions created by Zambada's arrest did not reconcile; the network did not heal.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper analyzed the BACRIM 2020 signed network of Mexican criminal organizations through a four-stage pipeline of structural characterization, link prediction, targeted-removal simulation, and optimal targeting under rewiring. The alliance network is sparse, clustered above random baselines, and dominated by two hubs whose removal fragments the cooperation structure. Topology alone cannot predict alliance formation. Geography and shared enemies are the primary drivers, and only a model incorporating these contextual features achieves meaningful predictive performance. Targeted hub removal produces fragmentation consistent with theoretical predictions for heavy-tailed networks, and post-disruption rewiring simulations identify which new alliances are structurally most plausible. Events between 2024 and 2026 confirm that the nodes flagged as most central were the ones law enforcement targeted, and that their removal produced consequences the simulation anticipated.

Several limitations qualify these findings. The 2020 snapshot cannot capture how ties form or dissolve in response to enforcement actions, and the rewiring simulation assumes the same formation mechanisms apply after disruption. The data is necessarily incomplete, as covert relationships may go unrecorded and organizational boundaries are sometimes blurry \citep{sparrow1991application}. We treat each organization as a unitary actor, but internal factions (e.g., Los Chapitos vs.\ Los Mayos) may have distinct alliance structures, as the 2024 Sinaloa civil war demonstrated. All alliance edges are treated as equal, though the original weight data (number of active states) could proxy for tie strength and might produce different centrality rankings.

These limitations point toward productive extensions. Temporal analysis over multiple BACRIM snapshots would allow direct testing of link predictions. ERGMs or STERGMs could formalize the same hypotheses with richer generative assumptions, and signed-link prediction (forecasting both alliances and rivalries) is a natural next step. The optimal targeting framework could be extended to sequential multi-round operations with inter-round rewiring, better reflecting the iterative nature of real enforcement campaigns. Despite these open questions, a single static snapshot analyzed with standard network tools and a dyadic formation model identified where the network was most fragile and how it would reorganize, information that proved consistent with the disruption events that followed.
