% --- Abstract ---
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper analyzes the BACRIM 2020 signed network---150 Mexican criminal organizations, their alliances, and their rivalries---using centrality analysis, null-model comparison, link prediction, and targeted node-removal simulation. The alliance network is sparse, clustered above random baselines, and dominated by two hubs (CJNG and Sinaloa). A dyadic logistic model incorporating geography, shared rivalries, and specialty outperforms topology-only link prediction (AUC 0.73 vs.\ chance). Targeted removal of CJNG fragments the network into multiple disconnected components, consistent with theoretical predictions for heavy-tailed topologies. Events between 2024 and 2026---the arrest of El Mayo Zambada, the killing of El Mencho, and mass extraditions---track the structural vulnerabilities flagged by this static 2020 snapshot.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Context and Motivation}

Organized crime is a relational phenomenon. Alliances and rivalries among criminal organizations shape who controls territory, who moves product, and where violence concentrates. In Mexico, drug cartels form webs of cooperation and competition that have contributed to over 290,000 homicides and 175,000 disappearances between 2012 and 2021 \citep{inegi2022}. Understanding the structure of these webs is not merely an academic exercise---it informs disruption strategy, intelligence prioritization, and policy design.

The BACRIM 2020 dataset \citep{prietocuriel2023bacrim} captures a signed network of 150 Mexican criminal organizations as of 2020: positive ties represent alliances and negative ties represent rivalries. Unlike datasets centered on individual operatives, BACRIM operates at the organizational level, treating each cartel, cell, or local group as a node. This allows the study of macro-level cooperation patterns---who allies with whom, who fights whom, and which organizations serve as bridges or brokers.

We model the relational structure as a graph $G = (V, E)$, where vertices $V$ represent organizations and edges $E$ represent observed alliances or rivalries. This formalism captures brokerage, clustering, and potential future link formation---dimensions invisible to traditional organizational charts or case-by-case intelligence analysis \citep{sparrow1991application}. We supplement the network data with organizational specialties (primary criminal activities and drug portfolios) compiled from DEA threat assessments \citep{dea2024, dea2025}, Congressional Research Service reports, and InSight Crime profiles.

Events between 2024 and 2026---the arrest of Ismael ``El Mayo'' Zambada, the killing of CJNG leader ``El Mencho,'' and the US designation of both cartels as terrorist organizations---track the structural vulnerabilities identified from this 2020 snapshot with notable precision. We discuss these connections in Section~\ref{sec:recent}.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}, leftmargin=2.5em]
    \item What structural properties characterize the cartel alliance network, and how does it differ from random baselines?
    \item Which cartel pairs are most likely to form future alliances, and do non-topological features (geography, rivalry, specialty) improve prediction over topology alone?
    \item How does targeted removal of central hubs fragment the network, and how does the surviving network plausibly reorganize?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}

We construct a signed network from the raw BACRIM data enriched with specialty attributes from DEA and open-source intelligence; compare it against Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi and Barab\'asi--Albert null models; predict future alliances with both topological metrics and a dyadic logistic model that incorporates geography, rivalry, and specialty; simulate targeted hub removal and post-disruption rewiring; and validate the structural predictions against events from 2024--2026.

\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}
% ============================================================

\citet{morselli2009inside} established the framework for studying criminal organizations as networks, showing that degree heterogeneity, clustering, and brokerage reflect strategic coordination rather than randomness. \citet{sparrow1991application} argued that social network analysis provides law enforcement with tools superior to hierarchy-based organizational charts, while identifying incompleteness, fuzzy boundaries, and temporal dynamics as persistent challenges.

The BACRIM dataset contains both alliance and rivalry ties, making it a signed network. The theoretical basis comes from \citet{heider1946attitudes} and the graph-theoretic formalization by \citet{cartwright1956structural}: in a balanced network, the enemy of my enemy is my friend. \citet{leskovec2010predicting} extended balance theory to large-scale networks and found that real signed networks tend toward structural balance.

\citet{albert2000error} showed that scale-free networks are robust to random failures but vulnerable to targeted attacks on hubs. \citet{duijn2014relative} applied these ideas to criminal networks, finding that targeting brokers (high betweenness) is often more effective than targeting hubs (high degree), because broker removal fragments communication channels even when fewer edges are lost.

\citet{libennowell2007link} established the framework for topological link prediction using common neighbors, Jaccard coefficient, and Adamic-Adar index. \citet{berlusconi2016link} applied these metrics to Italian Mafia co-offending networks and found that topological predictions were later confirmed by intelligence agencies.

\citet{prietocuriel2023bacrim} provided the first comprehensive signed network of Mexican cartel alliances and rivalries at the organizational level. \citet{coscia2012knowing} analyzed cartel territorial evolution over time. \citet{calderon2015beheading} studied how Mexico's kingpin strategy produced fragmentation that paradoxically increased violence---directly relevant to our node-removal analysis.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}\label{sec:data}

We use five data files. Three come from the BACRIM 2020 dataset \citep{prietocuriel2023bacrim}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Nodes.csv} --- 150 cartel organizations with group name, state, and short name.
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Alliances.csv} --- Alliance edges with positive weights (weight = number of Mexican states where the alliance is active).
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Rivals.csv} --- Rivalry edges with negative weights.
\end{itemize}

\noindent We supplement these with:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Nodes\_With\_Specialties.csv} --- Enriched node data produced by joining \texttt{BACRIM2020\_Nodes.csv} and \texttt{Cartel\_Specialties.csv} (via \texttt{join\_specialties.py}), adding organizational type (National Cartel, Regional Cartel, Local, Cell, Toll-Collector), primary specialty, activity list, drug portfolio, and row-level source attribution (e.g., DEA NDTA 2024/2025, CRS report R41576, and InSight Crime profiles \citep{dea2024,dea2025,crsR41576,insightcrimeProfiles}).
    \item \texttt{Trends2012\_2021.csv} --- Annual counts of homicides, missing persons, and arrests in Mexico from INEGI.
\end{itemize}

From these files we construct two graphs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item A \textbf{signed undirected master graph} where each alliance edge carries weight $+1$ and each rivalry edge carries weight $-1$.
    \item An \textbf{alliance-only subgraph} used for centrality, link prediction, and resilience tasks.
\end{enumerate}

Edge deduplication is handled by canonicalizing each edge as $(\min(u,v), \max(u,v))$. Isolate nodes (organizations with no alliance ties) are removed from the alliance subgraph for structural analysis.

\subsection{Centrality Measures}\label{sec:centrality_methods}

We compute two complementary centrality measures on the alliance graph.

For a node $v$ in an undirected graph with $N$ nodes, degree centrality normalizes the number of incident edges by the maximum possible:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:degree}
    C_D(v) = \frac{k_v}{N - 1}
\end{equation}
where $k_v = \sum_{j} A_{vj}$. High degree centrality identifies \emph{hubs}---organizations allied with many others. Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on shortest paths between other pairs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betweenness}
    C_B(v) = \sum_{\substack{s \neq v \neq t \\ s, t \in V}} \frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{st}$ is the total number of shortest paths from $s$ to $t$ and $\sigma_{st}(v)$ is the number passing through $v$. We normalize by $\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}$ and use Brandes' algorithm \citep{brandes2001faster}. High betweenness identifies \emph{brokers}---organizations that bridge otherwise disconnected clusters. A node ranking high on both measures is both highly connected and structurally irreplaceable; a node with high betweenness but moderate degree represents a hidden vulnerability.

\subsection{Null Models}\label{sec:null_models}

We compare the empirical alliance network against two null models matched on $N$ and (expected) $E$.

In the Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi $G(N,p)$ model, each of the $\binom{N}{2}$ possible edges exists independently with probability $p = 2E_{\text{real}} / (N(N-1))$, producing a Poisson degree distribution and expected clustering $C_{\text{ER}} = p$, which is small for sparse graphs \citep{erdos1959random}. In the Barab\'asi--Albert model, each new node attaches to $m$ existing nodes with probability proportional to current degree, producing a power-law distribution $P(k) \sim k^{-3}$; we set $m = \text{round}(E/N)$ to match the average degree \citep{barabasi1999emergence}. Following \citet{watts1998collective}, we compute the small-world coefficient:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:smallworld}
    \sigma = \frac{C_{\text{real}} / C_{\text{ER}}}{L_{\text{real}} / L_{\text{ER}}}
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the global clustering coefficient and $L$ is the average shortest path length in the largest connected component. A value $\sigma > 1$ indicates small-world properties: high local clustering combined with short global paths.

\subsection{Link Prediction}\label{sec:linkpred_methods}

We predict future alliances among currently non-adjacent pairs using two topological similarity metrics \citep{libennowell2007link}.

The Jaccard coefficient for non-adjacent nodes $u$ and $v$ measures the fraction of their combined neighborhood that is shared:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jaccard}
    J(u, v) = \frac{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)|}{|\mathcal{N}(u) \cup \mathcal{N}(v)|}
\end{equation}
The Adamic-Adar index weights each shared neighbor inversely by the log of its degree, so that low-degree shared neighbors contribute more \citep{adamic2003friends}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adamicadar}
    AA(u, v) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{N}(u) \cap \mathcal{N}(v)} \frac{1}{\log |\mathcal{N}(w)|}
\end{equation}
Candidates are ranked by Jaccard (primary) and Adamic-Adar (secondary). Both metrics use topology alone. A more complete model treats each potential pair $(i,j)$ as a decision and combines multiple dyadic features in a logistic regression:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:logistic}
    P(\text{alliance} \mid i, j) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_{ij})}}
\end{equation}
The feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{ij}$ includes six terms: (1) SharedAllies---common neighbors in the alliance graph (triadic closure); (2) SharedRivals---common enemies in the signed graph (structural balance: shared enemies push toward alliance); (3) Complementarity---1 if $i$ and $j$ have different primary specialties (supply-chain logic: different capabilities attract); (4) SameState---1 if both operate in the same Mexican state (geographic proximity); (5) BalanceScore---sum over common signed neighbors of $+1$ if both ties have the same sign, $-1$ otherwise (Heider balance); (6) DegreeProduct---$\deg(i) \times \deg(j)$ (preferential attachment). With 91 edges among $\binom{91}{2} = 4{,}095$ possible pairs (2.2\% positive rate), we use class-weighted logistic regression to prevent trivial all-zero predictions.

We compare four models using 5-fold edge-holdout cross-validation: (1) Jaccard alone, (2) Adamic-Adar alone, (3) logistic with topology features only (SharedAllies + DegreeProduct), (4) logistic with all 6 features. In each fold, 20\% of alliance edges are removed and AUC-ROC is computed on the held-out edges plus an equal number of randomly sampled non-edges.

After removing CJNG (Section~\ref{sec:resilience_methods}), we recompute all 6 dyadic features on the surviving network and score every non-adjacent pair with the logistic model fitted on the intact network. The top-ranked pairs are the most plausible new alliances.

\subsection{Network Resilience: Targeted Node Removal}\label{sec:resilience_methods}

For single targeted removal, we select the highest-degree/betweenness node $v^*$, remove it and all incident edges, prune new isolates, and compare pre- and post-removal metrics ($N$, $E$, density, clustering, components, LCC size, diameter, average path length).

For sequential targeted attack, we set $G_0 = G$ and at each step $t = 1, \ldots, k$ recalculate degrees in $G_t$, remove the current highest-degree node, prune new isolates, and record the LCC size and component count. Degree is recalculated after each removal (adaptive attack), which is more realistic than simultaneous removal since law enforcement operations unfold sequentially.

\citet{albert2000error} showed that scale-free-like networks are robust to random failure but fragile under targeted attack. The critical removal fraction is $f_c = 1 - 1/(\kappa - 1)$, where $\kappa = \langle k^2 \rangle / \langle k \rangle$; for heavy-tailed distributions, $\kappa$ is large and targeted removal rapidly causes collapse. We equate node removal with organizational neutralization, use the alliance-only graph, assume no rewiring after removal, prune isolates, and treat edges as unweighted.

\subsection{Optimal Targeting Under Rewiring}\label{sec:optimal_methods}

The resilience analysis treats node selection and post-disruption reorganization as separate steps. But if the goal is to choose which organizations to target, the two must be considered jointly. We formalize this as a combinatorial optimization problem. Let $S \subseteq V$ with $|S| = k$ be the set of nodes removed simultaneously, where $k$ is the operational budget. After removal, surviving organizations form new alliances according to the logistic model. The objective is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal}
    S^* = \arg\min_{S \subseteq V,\; |S| = k} \; \text{LCC}\Big(\text{Rewire}\big(G \setminus S,\; \hat{P},\; \tau\big)\Big)
\end{equation}
The function $\text{Rewire}(G', \hat{P}, \tau)$ takes the surviving graph $G'$ (after pruning isolates), scores every non-adjacent pair with the logistic model, adds the top edges whose count equals $\alpha \cdot E_{\text{lost}}$, and returns the reorganized graph. The parameter $\alpha \in \{0.25, 0.50, 0.75\}$ represents rewiring capacity---the fraction of lost edges the network can replace. Low $\alpha$ models a hostile enforcement environment; high $\alpha$ models a permissive one.

For $k = 1$, there are 91 candidate removals. Each evaluation involves scoring $\sim N^2/2$ non-edges with the logistic model, which takes milliseconds. We solve the problem exactly by exhaustive enumeration. We compare the optimal set against four baselines: top-$k$ by degree, top-$k$ by betweenness, degree-based with rewiring, and the mean over 200 random $k$-subsets.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Interpretation of Findings}

CJNG and Sinaloa hold the network together, allied with roughly half the non-isolate nodes. Their rivalry (weight = 7, the dataset maximum) defines the principal axis of conflict, creating a bipolar structure where most smaller organizations are satellites of one hub or the other. Yet several local organizations (Talibanes, Uni\'on Tepito, El Abuelo, Viagras) rank high on betweenness despite few alliances, occupying bottleneck positions whose disruption would fragment communication channels in ways that degree alone does not predict \citep{duijn2014relative}.

The alliance network connects organizations with different criminal portfolios---national cartels ally with local extortion groups, toll-collectors, and cells---suggesting functional complementarity. Cross-state alliances link production regions to distribution corridors, partly mapping supply-chain relationships. The BACRIM 2020 snapshot was taken during a period of nearly 37,000 annual homicides, coinciding with cartel fragmentation under the kingpin strategy. \citet{calderon2015beheading} documented the mechanism: decapitation creates power vacuums that produce violence. Our node-removal results quantify structural damage but do not capture the violence that fills the vacuum. Fragmentation is not pacification.

\subsection{Connection to Recent Events (2024--2026)}\label{sec:recent}

Events since 2020 track the structural vulnerabilities we identified with uncomfortable precision.

Ismael ``El Mayo'' Zambada, co-founder of the Sinaloa Cartel (the second-highest degree node in the 2020 network), was arrested in Texas on July 25, 2024, and pled guilty to narcotics and money laundering charges in August 2025. The arrest triggered a civil war between Los Chapitos and Zambada loyalists---in network terms, one high-degree node split into competing lower-degree nodes, with alliance edges replaced by rivalry edges as former collaborators took sides.

On February 22, 2026, Mexican military forces killed Nemesio Oseguera Cervantes (``El Mencho''), founder and leader of CJNG---the highest-degree and highest-betweenness node in the 2020 network. Our simulation predicts that removing CJNG drops the LCC from 50 to 26 and turns 23 satellite organizations into isolates. Within hours, suspected CJNG operatives launched revenge attacks in Jalisco (vehicle bombs, transit blockades), and the US Consulate in Guadalajara issued a shelter-in-place order. Whether CJNG's alliance network fragmented as the model predicts remains an empirical question, but the immediate destabilization is consistent with hub removal in a centralized network.

In February 2025, the US designated both CJNG and Sinaloa as Foreign Terrorist Organizations---the two dominant hubs, linked by a rivalry tie with weight 7 (the dataset maximum). Mass extraditions followed (29 figures in February 2025, 26 more in August 2025), and the dismantling of Los Cuinis---CJNG's strongest ally (alliance weight = 6)---further weakened the hub before El Mencho's elimination. The factions created by Zambada's arrest did not reconcile; the network did not heal.

\subsection{Limitations}

The main limitation is temporal: the 2020 snapshot cannot capture how ties form, dissolve, or shift in response to enforcement actions. The rewiring simulation assumes that the same formation mechanisms apply after disruption, which may not hold. The data is necessarily incomplete---covert relationships may go unrecorded and organizational boundaries are sometimes blurry \citep{sparrow1991application}. We treat each organization as a unitary actor, but internal factions (e.g., Los Chapitos vs.\ Los Mayos) may have distinct alliance structures, as the 2024 Sinaloa civil war demonstrated. Finally, we treat all alliance edges as equal; the original weight data (number of active states) could proxy for tie strength and might produce different rankings.

\subsection{Future Work}

Temporal analysis over multiple BACRIM snapshots would allow direct testing of link predictions. ERGMs or STERGMs could formalize the same hypotheses, and signed-link prediction---forecasting both alliances and rivalries---is a natural extension. The optimal targeting framework could be extended to sequential multi-round operations with inter-round rewiring.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

The cartel alliance network is sparse ($\rho = 0.022$), clustered above random baselines ($C = 0.036$ vs.\ $C = 0$ for both null models), and dominated by two hubs: CJNG (degree centrality 0.31, betweenness 0.27) and Sinaloa (0.23, 0.07). It does not match either null model---it clusters more than Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, is more fragmented than Barab\'asi--Albert, and has shorter average paths than both.

Topological link prediction alone performs at chance level. The dyadic logistic model, incorporating geography and shared rivalries alongside topology, reaches AUC 0.73---alliance formation is driven more by where organizations are and who their enemies are than by who their current allies are.

Removing CJNG eliminates 31\% of edges, turns 23 organizations into isolates, and halves the largest connected component. The post-disruption rewiring simulation predicts which new alliances form in the surviving network, and the optimal targeting analysis identifies which target sets produce the most durable fragmentation when the network can reorganize.

Events between 2024 and 2026 confirm that the nodes flagged as most central in the 2020 snapshot were the ones law enforcement targeted, and that their removal produced consequences our simulation anticipated. A single static snapshot, analyzed with standard network tools and a dyadic formation model, identified where the network was most fragile and how it would reorganize.
